## 1. Orthography System

Williams used an English-based ad-hoc spelling system with no standardized orthography. He applied English sound-spelling conventions (seventeenth-century Early Modern English) to Narragansett, an Algonquian language with a radically different phoneme inventory. The system was designed to be usable by English colonists, not by Algonquianists — so it systematically collapses phonemic distinctions that English lacks.

### Nasalized Vowels

A critical feature of SNEA phonology: Proto-Algonquian *a: and *o: developed nasalized reflexes in Narragansett before certain consonants. Williams does not mark nasality directly; he uses nasal coda consonant digraphs to imply it.

| Williams | IPA | Notes |
|----------|-----|-------|
| an, aum, aun | /ã/ | Critical for nasalization (PA *a: → ą in some environments) |
| om, on | /õ/ | (PA *o: → ǫ) |
| ook | /o:k/, /o:kʷ/ | Long vowel cluster, may be nasalized in context |

The nasalization is phonemic: *n now* (yes) vs. *núnow* (no) — only nasality distinguishes them, and Williams collapses this distinction orthographically in related forms.

## 3. Key Consonant Rules

### Stops

SNEA languages neutralized the PA voicing distinction in stops. Williams' English ear interpreted this as a gradient between voiced and voiceless, writing both `p` and `b` (though `b` is rare), `t` and `d` (ditto), `k` and `g` (nearly absent). In almost all cases, `p`, `t`, `k` represent an unaspirated stop — between English /b/ and /p/, /d/ and /t/, /ɡ/ and /k/.

| Williams | IPA | Notes |
|----------|-----|-------|
| p, pp | /b/ ~ /p/ [p] | Unaspirated lenis stop; alternates freely with `b` |
| t | /d/ ~ /t/ ~ /tʲ/ | Complex — before /i/ may palatalize to /tʃ/ |
| tt | /t/ ~ /d/ ~ /tʲ/ | Geminate, also palatalized in palatal contexts |
| k, kk | /k/ ~ /kʷ/ ~ /x/ | May be labialized before /o/ /u/; guttural before /a/ |
| c, cc | /k/ ~ /kʷ/ | Before a/o/u = /k/; before e/i = /s/ (English soft-c convention) |

**Cowan (1973) on PA *-k- and *-hk- development:** Cowan demonstrated that PA *-k- (simple velar) and *-hk- (preaspirated velar) merged phonetically in Narragansett as /hk/ medially, but can sometimes be distinguished through the behavior of preceding vowels. PA *-k- shortens a preceding long vowel; PA *-hk- does not. This provides an indirect method for reconstructing preaspiration from vowel length patterns — even though Williams never writes the /h/.

### CRITICAL: The Silent /h/

**English speakers routinely fail to hear preaspirated /h/ before stops.** This is the single most important finding for this source. Williams almost never wrote /h/ where modern reconstructions put preaspiration:

- In Narragansett (and other Southern New England Algonquian languages), /h/ commonly precedes /p/, /t/, /k/ in word-medial position (e.g., /hkaː/, /hp/, /ht/)
- English speakers perceive these as single stops /k/, /p/, /t/ — preaspiration is not a phonemic distinction in English
- Williams wrote `k` where the actual pronunciation was /hk/ (preaspirated k)
- **Confirmed by `\ph` records:** The 3 records with `\ph` fields show missing /h/ in Williams

Cowan (1973) further shows that PA preaspirated clusters *-hp-, *-ht-, *-hk- merge with simple stops in Williams' orthography but can sometimes be distinguished through the preceding vowel nucleus behavior. In some contexts, preaspiration triggers compensatory lengthening that Williams DOES capture.

#### Evidence from the 3 `\ph` entries in the DB

|----|----------|----------------|---------------------|
| 3834 | Aaunchemókaw | ąːčimoːhkaːw | `k` → hk (preaspirated) |

| 4285 | Anakáusu | anoːhkaːsəw | `k` → hk |

All three show the same pattern: Williams writes `k` where the reconstruction has /hk/. The /h/ is invisible to English ears.

## 4. Stress & Accent

Williams' orthography is **stress-marked**, unlike Eliot's Massachusett — this is a major advantage. Eliot's Massachusett Bible (1663) uses no accent marks, making stress reconstruction for Massachusett far more difficult. Williams' accents provide critical data about Narragansett prosody.

| Mark | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| á | Primary stress (or high pitch — debated) |
| à | Low/falling tone (secondary stress rarely written) |
| â | Rising-falling tone (in Narragansett; in Massachusett = nasal) |

**Aubin's Ojibway-like stress rule** (from personal correspondence):
1. All long vowels are stressed
2. All alternate even-numbered short vowels are stressed

But this rule requires correct vowel length classification, which hasn't been achieved for extinct SNEA languages. The problem is circular: we need vowel length to assign stress, but stress is one of the cues for vowel length.

Costa (2007) discusses the prosodic structure of SNEA languages and notes that Narragansett stress patterns appear similar to Loup A and Massachusett, with a preference for stress on long syllables and alternating rhythm on short syllables — but the documentary record is too thin to confirm this definitively.

## 5. Cross-Linguistic Cognate Evidence

### Massachusett (Eliot, 1663)

Massachusett, the closest sister language to Narragansett, was documented by John Eliot about 20 years after Williams. Eliot used a **different** orthographic convention — less English-phonetic, more systematic (he devised it for Bible translation). Key differences:

| Feature | Williams (Narragansett) | Eliot (Massachusett) |
|---------|------------------------|---------------------|
| Stress marking | Yes (acute, grave, circumflex) | No |
| Nasalization | Implied (an, aun, om) | Explicit (ô, û, or overline) |
| Stop voicing | Writes both p/b, t/d, k/g | More consistent p, t, k |
| /š/ | sh | sh (same) |
| /č/ | ch, tch | ch, tch |
| Preaspiration | Never written | Sometimes written h (rare) |

Goddard & Bragdon (1988) provides the definitive treatment of Massachusett phonology, including Part II pp. 474–486 on the orthography, which is directly transferable to Narragansett phonology. The phoneme inventory of Massachusett closely matches Narragansett:

Massachusett consonants: /p, t, č, k, s, š, h, m, n, w, j/ + clusters /hp, ht, hč, hk, hs, hš, hm, hn, hw, hj, mp, nt, nč, nk, ns, nš, sk, šk, sp, st, št, sw/
Massachusett vowels: /i, iː, o, oː, a, aː, ə, ã, õ/ — essentially identical to Narragansett

### Mahican (Edwards, 1787)

Jonathan Edwards (the younger) recorded Mahican, a Western New England Algonquian language, in the 1780s. Mahican is somewhat more distantly related but shares PA heritage. Edwards, being a trained theologian with some linguistic awareness, produced more systematic transcriptions.

Key Mahican comparison points:
- Edwards marked vowel length more consistently than Williams
- Mahican retained PA *r which Narragansett shifted to /n/ (PA *niːra → Narragansett *neen*, Mahican *niir*)
- Mahican final vowel deletion is more advanced than Narragansett
- Mahican kept PA *θ as /θ/ (written th) where Narragansett shifted to /n/

### Mohegan-Pequot (Prince & Speck, 1904)

J. Dyneley Prince and Frank Speck recorded Mohegan-Pequot in the early 1900s, the last documented SNEA language before extinction. This is our closest witness to what Narragansett may have sounded like 250 years after Williams, but with massive language shift already underway.

Key Mohegan-Pequot comparison:
- Prince & Speck used a rough English orthography similar to Williams, 260 years later
- Shows the same preaspiration blindness: /h/ before stops unwritten
- Many cognates with Williams' forms, showing the expected sound changes

Examples:
- Mohegan *wetu* (house) = Williams *wétu* = Massachusett *wétu* — shows remarkable stability of this noun across 250+ years
- Mohegan *nip* (water) = Williams *nipp* — stable
- Mohegan *ayum* (dog) — shows /ɬ/ → /j/ shift confirming PA *aɬemwa development

### Comparative Challenges

The primary challenge is that each recorder used a different English orthographic convention, reflecting both the recorder's dialect (17th c. London for Williams, 19th c. Boston for Eliot, 20th c. academic IPA approximation for Prince & Speck) and the stage of English orthographic history. Cross-recorder comparison requires normalizing through PA reconstructions, not direct orthographic matching.

## 6. Problematic / Reanalyzed Mappings

Several Williams spellings have been reanalyzed by modern scholars:

### `t` / `d` merger and palatalization

Williams writes `t` and `d` in free variation for what is likely a single phoneme /t/ with allophonic palatalization before /i/ and /j/. Early analysis assumed two separate phonemes; modern consensus (Goddard, Costa) favors a single phoneme with contextual voicing.

### `c` before front vowels

Williams uses `c` (English soft-c) for /s/ before e, i — e.g., *céppit* "hold" → actually /seːpit/. This is purely orthographic (English convention) and does NOT represent any distinct phoneme.

### Word-final vowels

Williams often writes a final `e` or `oh` that may represent:
- A genuine final /ə/ (schwa)
- A vestigial final vowel from PA
- English orthographic convention with no phonetic value

This affects at least 15-20% of entries. Comparison with Massachusett cognates (Eliot) is the primary disambiguation strategy.

### `sh` before consonants

Williams writes `sh` before consonants where Massachusett cognates suggest plain /s/:
- Williams *shp-* corresponds to Massachusett *sp-*
- This may indicate a real Narragansett innovation (palatalization of /s/ before certain consonants) or simply Williams using `sh` as a general sibilant marker

Costa (2007) notes that this palatalization before consonants is also attested in Loup A records, suggesting it is a genuine SNEA dialect feature, not a Williams idiosyncrasy.

### The `an`/`aun` ambiguity for /ã/

The most consequential ambiguity: Williams writes both `an` and `aun` for /ã/, but also writes `an` for plain /an/ (oral a + n). Disambiguating requires:
1. Checking the cognate in Massachusett (Eliot marks nasal /ô/ explicitly)
2. Checking PA reconstruction (do expected reflexes predict nasalization in this environment?)
3. Checking morphological structure (is the `n` part of a suffix, or a nasalization marker?)

## 7. Revitalization Context

Narragansett went extinct as a first language in the late 18th–early 19th century. Since the late 20th century, there have been revitalization efforts drawing primarily on:

- **Aubin's Proto-Algonquian reconstructions** as a starting point for phonological interpretation
- **Goddard & Bragdon (1988)** and **Bragdon (2009)** for Massachusett phonology transferable to Narragansett
- **Costa (2007)** for the SNEA dialect continuum and isoglosses distinguishing Narragansett from Massachusett
- **Williams' 2,134 lines** as the single largest Narragansett textual corpus
- **Hagenau (1962)** for morphological analysis of Williams' verb forms

### Current Revitalization Resources

- **The Narragansett Tribe's language program** uses modern pedagogical materials based on reconstructions from Williams. The chief challenge is that most published materials rely on optimistic phonological interpretation — extrapolating full phonology from 2,000 lines written by someone who could not hear the distinctions.
- **Waabu O'Brien's materials** (referenced above) are widely used in SNEA language circles. O'Brien compiled practical pronunciation guides that simplify the charted possibilities to one recommended pronunciation per mapped spelling.
- **The problem of underdetermination:** With only 2,134 records and only 3 with phonetic transcription (`\ph`), many phonological questions about Narragansett cannot be answered for certain. The practical revitalization approach is to adopt consistent conventions, while the scholarly approach requires flagging uncertainty.

### Open Questions for Revitalization

1. **Vowel length:** Was Narragansett vowel length phonemic (as in Massachusett)? Williams' orthography suggests yes (áh vs. a for long vs. short /a/), but the marking is inconsistent.
2. **Preaspiration:** How pervasive was /h/ before stops? The 3 `\ph` entries suggest it was common. If so, >50% of Williams' stops may be missing a preceding /h/.
3. **Nasalization phonemicity:** Was nasalization phonemic (as in Massachusett) or allophonic? Costa (2007) suggests phonemic in at least some SNEA dialects.
4. **Final vowel status:** Did Narragansett drop word-final vowels (like Mahican) or retain them (like Massachusett)? Williams' variable practice suggests either retention with devoicing or transition state.

## 8. Known Ambiguities (Summary)

1. **Vowel quality is highly ambiguous** — Williams' `a`, `e`, `i`, `o`, `u` each map to 2-4 possible phonemes
2. **Nasalization is implied but not marked** — `an`, `aun`, `om` etc. may or may not indicate nasal vowels depending on context
3. **Stop voicing is neutralized** — `p` = /b/~/p/, `t` = /d/~/t/, `k` = /g/~/k/
4. **English spelling conventions** — English digraphs (ea, oo, ch, sh) follow English values, but inconsistently due to 17th c. English pronunciation being different from modern English
5. **Silent e** — Word-final `e` is often silent, but not always
6. **Preaspiration /h/** — Almost never written, must be inferred from morphology
7. **Seventeenth-century English phonology** — Williams' spelling reflects his own Early Modern English pronunciation (e.g., `ea` in 1643 was /ɛː/, not modern /iː/), which differs from both modern English and modern Algonquianist expectations

## 9. Recommended Approach

**DO NOT USE REGEX** — the ambiguities are fundamentally context-dependent. Recommended:

1. **Finite State Machine (FSM)**: Encode known spelling→phoneme rules as weighted state transitions, with fallback probabilities for ambiguous mappings
2. **ML classifier**: Train on the 3 `\ph` seed examples (plus indirect evidence from comparative Algonquian) to predict preaspiration placement and vowel quality
3. **Morphological analysis**: Use known Narragansett morphology (prefixes, stems, suffixes from Aubin/Hagenau) to segment Williams' spellings before applying conversion
4. **Comparative evidence**: Use Proto-Algonquian reconstructions and sister languages (Massachusett, Mohegan-Pequot) as priors for ambiguous mappings

The 3 `\ph` entries alone are insufficient for ML training. Recommendation:
- Hand-annotate ~50 representative entries with conversions
- Use the annotations as training data
- Evaluate on the remaining entries with spot-check verification
- Cross-validate against Massachusett cognates (Goddard & Bragdon 1988) for at least 200 entries to calibrate the Narragansett-specific isoglosses

## 11. Related Work: Colonial Recorder Perception Problem

The core challenge of this research card — English-speaking recorders misperceiving and mis-transcribing Algonquian phonemes — is a known phenomenon documented across Eastern North America. Two researchers' work is directly relevant:

**Dr. Keith Cunningham** (Linguist, Nanticoke Indian Tribe). His doctoral dissertation *A Phonological Analysis of Nanticoke With Practical Applications for Language Revitalization* (Georgetown University) analyzes three 18th-century Nanticoke word lists (Heckewelder 1785) using the same methodology: historical phonology from colonial recorders, with the same challenges of multilingual informants and orthographic complexity. His 2024 Algonquian Conference presentation *A Revised Phonological Analysis of the Heckewelder Vocabulary of Nanticoke* directly parallels the Williams analysis — both involve English-speaking recorders working without standardized orthography, both require PA comparative calibration, and both feed into practical language revitalization. Cunningham's work demonstrates that the colonial recorder perception problem extends beyond SNEA into the broader Eastern Algonquian family (Nanticoke, Maryland Algonquian).

**Dr. Craig Kopris** (Independent scholar). His paper *Les sons wyandots perçus par des oreilles étrangères* (Wyandot sounds perceived by foreign ears, *Recherches amérindiennes au Québec*, 2014) is the exact framing of the Williams problem: how European recorders systematically misperceived and mis-transcribed indigenous phonemes. His *Wyandot Phonology: Recovering the Sound System of an Extinct Language* applies the same recovery methodology to an unrelated language family (Iroquoian), demonstrating that the foreign-ear perception problem is universal across colonial documentation of North American languages. His Dictionary of Wyandot project (NEH award FN-255576-17) shows the long-term application of these methods to language revitalization.

The Williams Narragansett corpus (2,134 records, only 3 with phonetic transcription) represents one of the most extreme cases of this problem — but the methodological framework for addressing it is established in the parallel work of Cunningham (Algonquian) and Kopris (Iroquoian).

## Cross-Linguistic Reassessment

### Dialect Classification and the Cree Chain Model

The card's classification of Narragansett as an N-dialect (PA *l → /n/) is correct as far as it goes, but Watkins's Cree documentation (1865) reveals a more nuanced picture. Watkins documented the l/n/th/y dialect chain across Cree — a single language where PA *l surfaces as /l/, /n/, /θ/, or /j/ depending on dialect, with intermediate zones showing gradient variation. This is not a categorical split but a continuum. Applied to SNEA, the rigid N-dialect/Y-dialect/R-dialect/L-dialect classification (Costa 2007) may overstate the discreteness of these divisions. The Cree evidence suggests that PA *l → /n/ in Narragansett and PA *l → /y/ in Mohegan may represent endpoints of a former continuum across Long Island Sound, with intermediate varieties (Loup A, the Connecticut River Valley languages) showing transitional reflexes. The "N-dialect" label for Narragansett should be understood as a statistical tendency, not an absolute boundary — and the conversion system should allow for gradient outcomes rather than enforcing categorical splits.

### The Sauk Vowel System as a PA Model

The card's vowel ambiguity analysis (Williams's a/e/i/o/u mapping to 2–4 phonemes each) is thorough, but the Sauk Dictionary (2005) provides a crucial missing piece: a clean four-vowel-length system (short a/e/i/o, long â/ê/î/ô) that directly reflects the underlying PA vowel inventory. Sauk preserves the PA length contrast transparently, unlike the SNEA languages where length became entangled with quality shifts and nasalization. This means that for any Narragansett form with a known PA etymon, the Sauk reflex provides a direct witness to the original length contrast — uncontaminated by the quality mergers that obscure the Williams orthography. The conversion FSM should incorporate Sauk reflexes as a tiebreaker for the a/e/i/o ambiguity: when Williams writes `a` and the Sauk cognate shows short /a/, the Narragansett reflex is likely /ə/; when Sauk shows long /â/, the reflex is likely /aː/ or /ɔː/. This gives the FSM a principled disambiguation strategy beyond the circular stress-length problem identified in §4.

### Recorder Reliability and the Brinton Lenape Parallel

The card's assessment of Williams's reliability — an English speaker who could not hear preaspiration, neutralized stop voicing, and collapsed vowel distinctions — is accurate but incomplete without the Brinton Lenape (1888) comparison. Brinton's Lenape data, recorded in a German-based Moravian orthography, shows the same b/p alternation, th/s alternation, and glottal-like terminal -g that Williams's orthography exhibits for Narragansett. Critically, Brinton documents the Unami/Minsi dialect split within a single orthographic tradition, demonstrating that orthographic variation across recorders does not necessarily reflect phonological variation in the language — it may reflect different recorder dialects of English (or German). Williams's 17th-century London English is a specific filter: his `ea` spellings reflect Early Modern English /ɛː/ (not modern /iː/), his `sh` before consonants may reflect his own sibilant perception rather than Narragansett phonology. The Brinton parallel suggests that some of what we attribute to Williams's "unreliability" may instead be systematic features of his idiolect that can be modeled and reversed.

### Preaspiration and the Arapaho Register Evidence

The card's central finding — that Williams never writes preaspirated /h/ before stops — is confirmed and deepened by the Arapaho Dictionary (2012). Arapaho, the most phonologically divergent Algonquian language, shows that PA preaspiration can develop into register variation: formal/careful speech preserves preaspiration while everyday speech lenites or deletes it. If a similar register distinction existed in Narragansett — and the sociolinguistic context (Williams recording a living language spoken in daily use, not a ceremonial register) suggests it may have — then Williams's consistent omission of /h/ may reflect not just perceptual blindness but also the register he was exposed to. The Arapaho evidence introduces a sociolinguistic dimension absent from the card's purely perceptual analysis: preaspiration may have been variably present even in native speech, and Williams's transcriptions may accurately represent one register. The conversion system should therefore model preaspiration as probabilistic rather than categorical, with register as a latent variable.

### The Watkins s/sh Alternation and Narragansett Sibilants

Watkins's Cree documentation also records an s/sh alternation across Cree dialects that directly parallels the Narragansett `sh`-before-consonant problem identified in §6. In Cree, PA *s surfaces as /s/ in some dialects and /ʃ/ in others before consonants, with no change in meaning. The card notes that Williams writes `shp-` where Massachusett has `sp-`, and Costa (2007) treats this as a genuine Narragansett innovation. The Cree evidence supports Costa: sibilant palatalization before consonants is a recurrent Algonquian areal feature, not a recorder artifact. The conversion FSM should therefore map Williams's `sh` before consonants to /ʃ/ (not /s/), treating it as a real phonological feature of Narragansett rather than a Williams idiosyncrasy. This has downstream consequences for phoneme inventory: if Narragansett had /ʃ/ in pre-consonantal position where Massachusett had /s/, the two languages are more phonologically distinct than the card assumes.

### The OjibweLexicon and Stem Alternation Classes

The OjibweLexicon CSV (27,000+ entries) provides a resource the card does not leverage: stem alternation classes (w2, y2, 1, 2) that reflect historical PA stem-finals. In Ojibwe, these classes determine how stems behave in inflection — and they are directly traceable to PA *-w, *-y, and other stem-final consonants that were lost or modified in SNEA. For Narragansett, where Williams's transcriptions rarely preserve stem-final distinctions, the Ojibwe stem classes provide a principled way to reconstruct the underlying stem shape from the PA etymon. This is particularly valuable for verb forms, where Williams's English-based transcription often obscures the stem-suffix boundary. The conversion system should incorporate Ojibwe stem class information as a prior for morphological segmentation, reducing the ambiguity in Williams's verb forms that Hagenau (1962) struggled with.
