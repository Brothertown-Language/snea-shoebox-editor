# Orthography Research: Wampanoag/Massachusett Historical Source Vocabularies

## Scope & Purpose

This card catalogs the orthographic conventions and phonological encoding found in historical Wampanoag (Massachusett/Natick) source vocabularies relevant to the SNEA (Shoebox) editor project. The purpose is to document how each source encodes Wampanoag speech so that:
1. The parsing layer understands the orthographic fingerprint of each source
2. Differences between sources are understood as systematic orthographic variation, not chaotic misspellings
3. The unified orthography conversion can correctly map from any source spelling to a standard modern form

## Source 1: William Wood — *New Englands Prospect* (1634)

### Bibliographic Details

- **Full title**: *New Englands Prospect: A true, lively, and experimentall description of that part of America, commonly called Nevv England*
- **Author**: William Wood
- **Printer**: Tho. Cotes, London
- **Format**: Small 4to, contains a "small Nomenclator" of ~262 Massachusett words
- **ALR volume**: Volume 27 (Salvucci edition, pp. 252–261)
- **Etymology URL**: <http://www.evolpub.com/interactiveALR/database/pdfs/Vol._27_Massachusetts.pdf>
- **Digital transcription**: <http://people.umass.edu/aef6000/Texts/Algonquian/Massachusett/Alia/Wood1634>

### Orthographic Characteristics

Wood's vocabulary is the largest pre-Eliot Massachusett word list. Its orthography follows English sound-spelling conventions of the 1630s:

| Wood Spelling | Modern Massachusett | English Gloss |
|---|---|---|
| `Kean` | keen | I |
| `Keisseanchacke` | - | back of the hand |
| `Ksitta` | - | it hurts me |
| `Kawkenog wampompeage` | - | let me see money |
| `Kagmatcheu` | - | will you eat meat |
| `Wigwam` | wekou | a house |
| `Web` | - | a wife |
| `Sawup` | - | 1 sleep (numeral classifier) |
| `Wompey` | wompi | white |
| `Waacoh` | - | the day breaks |

**Key patterns:**
- **Initial /w-/ retained**: `Wigwam`, `Web`, `Wompey`
- **`<ea>` for /iː/**: `Kean` (cf. Eliot's `keen`)
- **`<s>` for /ʃ/ inconsistently**: `Sawup` where later sources might write `sh-`
- **`<k>` preferred over `<c>`**: Initial /k/ written `K-` even before front vowels (`Kean`, `Ksitta`)
- **No diacritics**: Wood uses plain English letters only
- **Vowel length not marked**: Short vs. long vowel distinction invisible
- **Pidginization noted**: Goddard (1977) observes a "good deal of pidginization" in Wood's data (Salvucci 2002)

### Linguistic Notes

- Wood's data shows influence from the Massachusetts Bay area
- The vocabulary is organized by semantic category (body parts, animals, trade goods, place names)
- The Nomenclator also includes month names, day-name classifiers, and a list of place names with English equivalents

## Source 2: Edward Winslow — *Good Newes from New England* (1624)

### Bibliographic Details

- **Full title**: *Good Newes from New England, or a True Relation of Things very Remarkable at the Plantation of Plimouth in New-England*
- **Author**: Edward Winslow
- **ALR volume**: Volume 27 (Salvucci edition, pp. 262–263)
- **Source for reprint**: Young's *Chronicles of the Pilgrim Fathers* (1841)

### Orthographic Characteristics

22 scattered words and phrases from the Plymouth (Pokanoket) area. Winslow himself describes the language as "very copious, large, and difficult" (Salvucci 2002).

**Key patterns:**
- Represents Pokanoket (southern Wampanoag) dialect, distinct from the Massachusett proper of Boston Bay
- Shows a "good deal of pidginization" (Goddard 1977)
- English-based orthography with no standardization
- Informants likely from the Pokanoket nation (Massasoit's people)

## Source 3: Anonymous / Thomas Shepard — *The Day-Breaking, if not the Sun-Rising of the Gospell with the Indians in New-England* (1647)

### Bibliographic Details

- **Full title**: *The Day-breaking, if not the sun-rising of the Gospell with the Indians in New-England*
- **Printer**: Rich. Cotes for Fulk Clifton, London, 1647
- **Collation**: [2], 25, [1] p. ; 4°
- **Authorship**: Anonymous; "To the Reader" signed by Nathan. Warde. Variously attributed to:
  - **John Wilson** (1588–1667) — pastor of Boston church
  - **Thomas Shepard** (1605–1649) — pastor of Cambridge church; attribution preferred by Sabin (per Sabin Americana 36893)
  - **John Eliot** (1604–1690) — "Apostle to the Indians"; less likely as author of this specific tract
  - The authorship question is discussed in *Proceedings of the Massachusetts Historical Society*, 1890/91, v. 26 (2nd ser., v. 6) pp. 392–395
- **ESTC**: R21203
- **Wing**: S3110
- **EEBO TCP**: A59662.0001.001
- **OTA identifier**: ota:A59662
- **ALR volume**: Volume 27 (Salvucci edition, pp. 262–263)
- **Digital full text**: <https://ota.bodleian.ox.ac.uk/repository/xmlui/handle/20.500.12024/A59662>
- **Digital source**: Transcribed by the Text Creation Partnership (TCP) from EEBO phase 1
- **Reprints**:
  - Sabin's Reprints, No. 9 (Joseph Sabin, New York, 1865) — source for Salvucci's transcription
  - In *Massachusetts Historical Society Collections*, 3rd series, vol. 4 (1834), pp. 1–27
  - Gale Ecco / Sabin Americana print-on-demand (2012), ISBN 978-1-275-85723-4

### Historical Context

This is the **first published account of John Eliot's missionary work** and the first printed record of his preaching in the Massachusett language. It describes four meetings with Wampanoag people:

| Meeting | Date | Location | Key Details |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1st | October 28, 1646 | Wigwam of Waaubon (Waban), near Watertown Mill | First sermon; 6 theological questions from Indians |
| 2nd | November 11, 1646 | Same wigwam | Catechizing children; old man's "too late" question; weeping conversions |
| 3rd | November 26, 1646 | Same location (more wigwams built) | Questions about praying to the Devil, humiliation, dreams |
| 4th | After November 26 | Wampas's report | Offer of children for English education; confessions |

**The sachems mentioned:**
- **Cutshamakin** (Cutchamakin) — Sachem of Neponset (near Dorchester)
- **Waban** (Waaubon) — "A mere sachem" along the Charles River near modern Newton
- These sachems belonged to the **Massachusett proper** nation

**Nonantum**: The location where "men, women, children gathered together from all quarters round about" and founded the first "Praying Indian" town. Modern location: Newton, Massachusetts.

### Linguistic Content: The 11 Words

Salvucci (2002) identifies **11 scattered native words and phrases** in this text, including two one-sentence prayers. Based on reading of the full EEBO/TCP text and Salvucci's ALR Volume 27 transcription:

| # | Word/Phrase (original spelling) | English gloss (in source) | Modern Massachusett (Trumbull) | Page in original |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | `Wigwam` | house/tent | wekou / wékou | passim |
| 2 | `Evangenesh Iehovah` | the Commandments of Jehovah | — | p. 6 |
| 3 | `musquantum` | very angry | musquantum | p. 13 |
| 4 | `Chechainuppan` | tormented alive | — | p. 14 |
| 5 | `Wowein wicke Iehovah` | live in all blisse with Iehovah | — | p. 14 |
| 6 | `Sachem` | King | sôchim | p. 20 |
| 7 | `Pawwaw` | Sorcerers and Witches | powwâw | p. 20 |
| 8 | `Chepian` | the devil | chepiân | p. 21 |
| 9 | `Noonatomen` | rejoycing | — | (per Salvucci) |
| 10 | `Amanaomen Iehovah tahassen metagh` | Take away Lord my Stony heart (prayer) | — | (per Salvucci) |
| 11 | `Cheehesom Iehovah kekowhogkew` | Wash Lord my soule (prayer) | — | (per Salvucci) |

**Note on the original 11-word list from ALR Volume 27**: The current card's sample words (`kean`, `kenomptom`, `wean`, `nukus`, `kodtomp`, `ompmom`, `sawop`, `acheta co`) do NOT match the words actually found in the 1647 text. These words appear to originate from **William Wood's 1634 vocabulary** (where `kean`, `sawup` are attested), not from *The Day-Breaking*. The ALR Volume 27 combines Wood (262 words), Winslow (22 words), and Shepard (11 words) in one PDF volume without explicit per-page attribution in the online sample, which likely caused the word list confusion in the original card. The correct Shepard word list is the 11 items above.

### Orthographic Characteristics

The Wampanoag words in this pamphlet show:

1. **Unstandardized English orthography**: Words are spelled as they sounded to English ears, with no consistent system
2. **`<ch>` for /č/**: `Chechainuppan`, `Chepian`, `Cheehesom`
3. **`<sh>` and `<s>` for /š/**: `Evangenesh`, `Iehovah` (given English biblical name spelling)
4. **`<w>` for /w/**: `Wigwam`, `Wowein`, `wicke`
5. **Final `-an` for animate nouns**: `Chepian`, `Chechainuppan`
6. **`<oo>` for /uː/**: `Noonatomen`
7. **`<p>` for /b/**: `Chepian` (cf. Eliot's `chepiân`)
8. **Preaspiration**: `cke` in `wicke` may indicate preaspirated stop

### The Two Prayers

The pamphlet contains the earliest recorded Wampanoag Christian prayers, reflecting Eliot's first attempts at composing religious texts in the language:

- **Prayer 1**: `Amanaomen Iehovah tahassen metagh` — "Take away Lord my Stony heart"
  - `Amanaomen` = "take away" (command)
  - `Iehovah` = Jehovah
  - `tahassen` = "stone/heart of stone"? (cf. Natick: `tahshan` = stone, rock)
  - `metagh` = "my heart" (cf. Natick: `məhtah`)

- **Prayer 2**: `Cheehesom Iehovah kekowhogkew` — "Wash Lord my soule"
  - `Cheehesom` = "wash" (command)
  - `kekowhogkew` = "my soul" (cf. Natick: `kekeau` = soul, spirit)

These prayers predate Eliot's published *Indian Grammar* (1666) by 19 years and his *Indian Primer* (1669) by 22 years. They represent Eliot's earliest work in the language, before he had fully analyzed its grammar.

### Relationship to the Eliot Tract Series

*The Day-Breaking* (1647) is the second in the series of "Eliot Tracts" (misleadingly named — Eliot was not necessarily the author):

| # | Title | Year | Author |
|---|---|---|---|

| 3 | *The Clear Sun-shine of the Gospel* | 1648 | Thomas Shepard |
| 4 | *The Glorious Progress of the Gospel* | 1649 | E. Winslow |
| 5 | *The Light Appearing* | 1651 | H. Whitfield |

## Source 4: John Eliot — *The Indian Grammar Begun* (1666)

### Bibliographic Details

- **Full title**: *The Indian Grammar Begun: Or, An Essay to bring the Indian Language into Rules, For the Help of such as desire to learn the same, for the furtherance of the Gospel among them*
- **Author**: John Eliot
- **Printer**: Marmaduke Johnson, Cambridge, Massachusetts
- **Format**: Small 8vo, ~66 pp.
- **Digital source**: Available through EEBO and Archive.org

### Orthographic Characteristics

Eliot's grammar is the **first published grammatical analysis of any Algonquian language**. It represents Eliot's mature understanding of Massachusett after ~20 years of study.

The orthographic system Eliot presents in the Grammar is a **deliberately constructed orthography**, not a casual English-based spelling:

| Eliot's Letter | Pronunciation | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| `a` | /a/ ~ /ə/ | |
| `e` | /iː/ | Always long; Eliot's distinctive feature |
| `i` | /ɪ/ ~ /i/ | |
| `o` | /o/ | |
| `u` | /ə/ ~ /ʌ/ | |
| `â` | /aː/ | Eliot's macron for long vowel |
| `ô` | /oː ~ uː/ | "oo" in English terms |
| `u` (with dieresis) | /ə/ | Schwa |
| `b` | /p/ | Unaspirated p (allophone) |
| `p` | /pʰ/ | Aspirated p |
| `k` | /kʰ/ | Aspirated k |
| `g` | /k/ | Unaspirated k (allophone) |
| `ch` | /č/ | |
| `sh` | /š/ | |
| `t` | /tʰ/ | Aspirated t |
| `d` | /t/ | Unaspirated t (allophone) |
| `w` | /w/ | |
| `y` | /j/ | |

**Key innovation**: Eliot distinguishes **aspirated vs. unaspirated stops** using English voiced/voiceless pairs (p~b, t~d, k~g). This is the most important orthographic feature for phonological reconstruction.

### Vowel System (from the Grammar)

Eliot describes 5 primary vowels with length distinctions:
- `a` (short) vs. `â` (long)
- `e` /iː/ (always long)
- `i` (short) vs. `ee`/`ie` (long — inconsistently marked)
- `o` (short) vs. `ô` (long)
- `u` (short schwa) vs. `oo` (long /uː/)

## Source 5: John Eliot — *Mamusse Wunneetupanatamwe Up-Biblum God* (1663)

### Bibliographic Details

- **Full title**: *Mamusse Wunneetupanatamwe Up-Biblum God Naneeswe Nukkone Testament kah wonk Wusku Testament* (The Holy Bible containing the Old Testament and the New Testament)
- **Printer**: Samuel Green and Marmaduke Johnson, Cambridge, Massachusetts
- **Format**: Quarto, ~1,200 pp.
- **Edition**: 1663 (first edition); 1685 (second edition, revised by Eliot)
- **Digital source**: Available through Archive.org, EEBO, and the John Carter Brown Library

### Orthographic Characteristics

Eliot's Bible translation is the **largest single Massachusett language corpus** and establishes the orthographic standard for all subsequent works. The orthographic system is more consistent than the Grammar in some respects, less in others.

**Distinctive features of Eliot Bible orthography:**

| Feature | Example | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Macron on long vowels** | `â`, `ô` | Used consistently for /aː/ and /oː~uː/ |
| **`ꝏ` (oo ligature)** | `wꝏ` (he) | Represents /uː/; a distinctive Eliot feature |
| **Final `-uh`** | `wosketomp-uh` | Noun suffix |
| **`-og` plural animate** | `wosketompp-og` | |
| **`-ash` plural inanimate** | `wetu-ash` | |
| **Distinctive punctuation** | Eliot uses a unique system | Full colons, half colons for grammatical pauses |

**However**: The Bible orthography is **not fully consistent even internally**. Ives Goddard and Kathleen Bragdon (1988) identify:
- Variation between `â` and `a` for the same morpheme in different contexts
- Inconsistent use of the `oo` vs. `ꝏ` distinction
- Variation in the marking of vowel length

## Source 6: Josiah Cotton — *Vocabulary of the Massachusetts (or Natick) Indian Language* (compiled 1707–1708, published 1829)

### Bibliographic Details

- **Compiler**: Josiah Cotton (1680–1756), grandson of John Cotton
- **Manuscript dates**: 1707–1708
- **First published**: 1829 in *Collections of the Massachusetts Historical Society*, 3rd series, vol. 2, pp. 147–257
- **Editor**: John Pickering
- **Separate issue**: Cambridge [Mass.], 1829
- **Digital**: Library of Congress (loc.gov/item/13011245); Archive.org (vocabularyofmass00cott)

### Orthographic Characteristics

Cotton's vocabulary was compiled about 40 years after Eliot's major publications and shows some evolution:

| Cotton's orthography | Corresponding Eliot form | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Wish | w | Cotton uses `wish` as a letter name but the text itself uses Eliot's orthography |
| Nôsh | nôsh | my father — Cotton agrees with Eliot |
| Chepiohkōmuk | chepiohkomuk | hell |
| Wosketomp | wosketomp | man |
| Mittamwo˘ssis | mittamwossis | woman |
| `˘` (breve) | — | Cotton uses breve for extra-short vowels |

**Cotton's key contributions:**
- **Extended Eliot's orthography** with additional diacritics (breve for ultra-short vowels)
- **Recorded early 18th-century pronunciation** shifts from Eliot's 17th-century data
- **Added hundreds of new lexical items** not in Eliot's Bible
- **Included dialogues and example sentences** not just word lists

### Structure

The vocabulary is organized by semantic category:
- Of Arts, Beasts, Birds, Rational Creatures, Faculties, Fish, Garments, Herbs, Husbandry, House, Household stuff, Meat, Metals, School, Senses, Ships, Time, Trees, Virtues & Vices
- Then by part of speech: Adjectives, Numbers, Pronouns, Verbs, Colloquial Phrases, Participles, Adverbs, Conjunctions, Interjections, Prepositions, Sentences
- Plus: Dialogues, Native primer extracts, Lord's Prayer in two versions, Ten Commandments, a Cotton sermon (1710)

## Source 7: Roger Williams — *A Key into the Language of America* (1643)

### Bibliographic Details

- **Full title**: *A Key into the Language of America: Or, An help to the language of the natives in that part of America called New-England*
- **Author**: Roger Williams
- **Printer**: Gregory Dexter, London
- **Format**: Small 8vo, ~200 pp. with ethnographic observations

### Orthographic Characteristics

Williams records **Narragansett** (not Massachusett), a closely related but distinct Southern New England Algonquian language. Narragansett is the Rhode Island counterpart to Massachusett.

Williams' orthography is the most phonetically sophisticated of the 17th-century sources:

| Williams | Sound | Example |
|---|---|---|
| `ò` | /ɔː/ | |
| `e` | /iː/ | |
| `ú` | /ə/ | |
| `ch` | /č/ | |
| `sh` | /š/ | |
| Accent marks | Stress | Used with fair consistency |

**Key distinction from Eliot**: Williams provides **minimal pairs and stress marks** that allow phonological reconstruction with greater certainty.

### Relationship to Massachusett Sources

Narragansett (Williams) and Massachusett (Eliot) are distinct but mutually intelligible dialects of the same language continuum. Key differences:
- Narragansett `s` corresponds to Massachusett `sh` in some environments
- Different vocabulary for some common items
- Narragansett preserves some features lost in Massachusett proper and vice versa

## Source 8: J. Hammond Trumbull — *Natick Dictionary* (1903)

### Bibliographic Details

- **Full title**: *Natick Dictionary: A New England Indian Lexicon*
- **Editor**: J. Hammond Trumbull (1821–1897)
- **Publication**: Bureau of American Ethnology, Bulletin 25, Washington, D.C. (1903)
- **Digital**: Widely available online (Archive.org, HathiTrust)

### Significance

Trumbull's *Natick Dictionary* is the **definitive 19th-century scholarly lexicon** of the Massachusett language. It standardizes and analyzes Eliot's orthography into a consistent dictionary form.

**Key features:**
- Headwords normalized using Trumbull's romanization (modified Eliot orthography)
- Extensive cross-references to Eliot's Bible and other sources
- Etymological analysis with cognates in related Algonquian languages
- Grammatical information (noun class, verb transitivity)

## Source 9: Ives Goddard & Kathleen Bragdon — *Native Writings in Massachusett* (1988)

### Bibliographic Details

- **Full title**: *Native Writings in Massachusett* (2 vols.)
- **Authors**: Ives Goddard and Kathleen J. Bragdon
- **Publisher**: American Philosophical Society, Philadelphia (1988)
- **Series**: Memoirs of the American Philosophical Society, vol. 185

### Significance

This is the **definitive modern scholarly edition** of Massachusett texts written by native speakers. It includes:
- **Transcriptions with modern phonological interpretation**
- **Linguistic analysis** of the Massachusett writing system as used by native writers
- **Historical context** for each document
- **The most reliable interpretation** of the orthographic system and its phonetic values

**Goddard & Bragdon's key orthographic insights:**
- Eliot's <b, d, g> represent unaspirated lenis stops (not voiced)
- Eliot's <p, t, k> represent aspirated fortis stops
- Vowel length is phonemic and is marked inconsistently in native writers
- The writing system was taught primarily through the Eliot Bible, so native writers inherited Eliot's orthographic conventions

### Orthographic Error Patterns by Source

Each source has characteristic "errors" or systematic deviations from a standardized phonemic representation:

| Source | Characteristic "Errors" |
|---|---|
| **Wood (1634)** | No vowel length; English digraphs predominate; treats unaspirated stops as voiced; pidginized forms |
| **Shepard (1647)** | No consistent system; words are ad-hoc English approximations; preaspiration inconsistently shown; <ck> for /k/ |
| **Eliot (1666+)** | Most consistent system; treats unaspirated stops as voiced English letters (b, d, g) but means unaspirated; vowel length inconsistently marked on some vowels |
| **Cotton (1708)** | Extends Eliot's system; adds breve; shows late shift in some vowels; more consistent vowel marking |
| **Trumbull (1903)** | Standardized historical reconstruction; marks length and aspiration consistently; normalizes variation |

## Implications for SNEA Processing

### Parsing Layer Requirements

The SNEA parser must:
1. **Detect source fingerprint**: Each source has a characteristic orthographic texture that should be identifiable
2. **Apply source-specific decoding**: The same letter sequence may have different phonetic values in different sources
3. **Resolve ambiguity**: Where a spelling could represent multiple phonemes (e.g., Wood's `<ea>` = /iː/ or /ɛː/), use source context and Algonquian comparative evidence

### Source-Adaptive Parsing Strategy

| Source | Strategy | Key Mapping |
|---|---|---|
| Wood | English sound-spelling → phoneme via lookup table | ea→/iː/, oo→/uː/, k→/k/, s→/s~š/ |
| Shepard | Ad-hoc English → phoneme via context | Same as Wood but with higher ambiguity |
| Eliot | Eliot orthography → phoneme via Grammar rules | p/b→/p/ vs. /pʰ/, t/d→/t/ vs. /tʰ/, k/g→/k/ vs. /kʰ/, â→/aː/, ô→/oː~uː/ |
| Cotton | Same as Eliot + breve → extra-short vowel | Above + vowel quantity more reliable |
| Trumbull | Modern normalized → phoneme directly | The cleanest mapping |

### Orthography Conversion Pipeline

For SNEA's unified orthography conversion:
1. **Source detection** → which source's orthographic fingerprint
2. **Source normalizer** → convert to Eliot-consistent form
3. **Eliot parser** → apply Grammar rules to phonemic representation
4. **Unified output** → standard modern transcription

This pipeline must handle the fact that some sources (Wood, Shepard) predate Eliot's system and are thus pre-orthographic, while others (Cotton, Trumbull) postdate it and may show phonological change.
