## 1. Fidelia Fielding and the Last Speaker Corpus

### 1.1 Fidelia A. H. Fielding (1827–1908)

Fidelia A. Hoscott Fielding (Mohegan name: **Jits Bodunaxa** "Flying Bird") was the last fluent speaker of the Mohegan-Pequot language. Born in 1827 at Mohegan, near Norwich, Connecticut, she learned Mohegan as a child from her grandmother, **Martha Uncas Shantup** (1761–1859), a direct descendant of Sachem Uncas. As the number of fluent speakers dwindled through the late 19th century, Fielding maintained her language by speaking with her sister and, later, by talking to herself (Prince & Speck 1904:18–19).

### 1.2 The Fielding Diaries

Between 1902 and 1904, Fielding kept a series of diaries written in the Mohegan language. These are the only known surviving documents produced by a native speaker of Mohegan-Pequot. The corpus consists of:

- **Three diaries** and a copy of the Lord's Prayer in Mohegan (Cornell University Library, acquired 2004 from the Huntington Free Library)
- A fourth diary was lost in a fire at Columbia University around 1906 (Granberry 2003:10)
- A sermon text published by Prince and Speck (1903) in *American Anthropologist*, vol. 5, pp. 193–212
- A long narrative tale recorded in phonetic notation by Frank Speck (Speck 1904)

The diaries were written in Fielding's own orthography — an English-based ad-hoc spelling system reflecting her pronunciation. After Fielding's death in 1908, the diaries passed to Frank Speck at the University of Pennsylvania, and later to Cornell University. In November 2020, Cornell repatriated the diaries to the Mohegan Tribe in a formal ceremony, where they now reside in the Mohegan Archives at Uncasville, Connecticut (Cornell University Library, Nov 2020).

### 1.3 Corpus Structure

The Brothertown corpus under analysis contains **145 records** from the Fielding dictionary. This is a subset of the full dictionary (which numbers several thousand entries, many reconstructed via comparative Algonquian methods by linguist David Costa). Records marked **FF** in the dictionary indicate words attested directly in Fidelia Fielding's diaries. The 145 records constitute our working corpus because they have explicit Mohegan-to-phoneme field mappings vs. the larger set which has modern orthography forms needing parsing.

### 1.4 Comparative Significance

Prince and Speck (1904:18) explicitly compare Fielding to **Dorothy Pentreath**, the last speaker of Cornish, noting the "strangely pathetic" nature of a dying language spoken only by a single elderly woman who kept it alive by talking to herself. Despite the "last stages of decay" they observed, they found that "enough of the original phonetics and grammatical phenomena" remained to judge the language's structure.

## 2. Stephanie Fielding's Grammatical Analysis

### 2.1 The Modern Mohegan Dictionary

**Stephanie Fielding** (Cits Pátunáhshô), a full-blooded Mohegan tribal member and descendant of Fidelia Fielding by marriage (her great-great-grandfather's aunt), served as the Mohegan Tribe's linguist and authored the *Modern Mohegan Dictionary* (2006, revised 2013). The dictionary was reviewed by David Costa (Myaamia Center, Miami University of Ohio) who:

- Researched the lexicon and grammar
- Developed the Mohegan alphabet
- Translated scripts and wrote sample sentences
- Proofed the grammar paradigms

The dictionary includes several sections beyond a word list:

1. **Pronunciation Guide** — the practical orthography with phoneme mappings
2. **Mohegan Grammar Paradigms** — a systematic grammatical sketch covering pronouns, nouns, verbs, and particles
3. **Mohegan to English Dictionary** — stems with part-of-speech codes and inflections
4. **English to Mohegan Word Finder** — English lookup with Mohegan stems

### 2.2 Grammatical Description

Fielding's grammar covers the major Algonquian grammatical categories:

- **Pronouns**: Prefixes and suffixes attached to verb stems (pro-drop / polysynthetic). Independent personal pronouns exist but are less common.
- **Nouns**: Two genders (Animate vs. Inanimate). Animate nouns include people, animals, celestial bodies, spirits, and some culturally significant items. Plural and obviative inflections, locative and absentative cases.
- **Verbs**: Four orders (Independent, Conjunct, Imperative, Participial) with modes including Indicative, Subjunctive, and various tense forms. Transitivity marked (VAI, VII, VTA, VTI).
- **Derivation**: Prefixes (especially person-marking: `nu-` 1st person, `ku-` 2nd) and suffixes mark person, number, tense, and mode.

### 2.3 Fielding's Methodology

Stephanie Fielding worked extensively from texts, particularly the **Eliot Bible** (John Eliot's 1663 Massachusett translation), using a concordance to find Wôpanâak cognates and applying Mohegan-specific sound changes to reconstruct Mohegan forms. This comparative method is standard for Algonquian language reclamation. She also drew from:

- Fidelia Fielding's diaries (primary source, marked FF)
- Sister languages (Wôpanâak, Narragansett, Munsee Delaware)
- Proto-Algonquian reconstructions with sound-law application
- David Costa's grammatical charts and comparative research

A detailed grammatical description of Mohegan was in preparation but the primary published source remains the 2006 dictionary (Wikipedia "Mohegan-Pequot Language").

### 2.4 Yale Presidential Visiting Fellowship

Stephanie Fielding spent the 2017–2018 academic year as a presidential visiting fellow at Yale University, teaching a course comparing the Mohegan and Delaware languages. She has also worked with the Unkechaug people of Long Island to help reclaim their nearly identical language (the primary difference being the Mohegan /y/ vs. Unkechaug /r/ correspondence).

## 3. Historical Phonology: Proto-Algonquian Reflexes

### 3.1 PA Vowel System

Proto-Algonquian is reconstructed with an eight-vowel system: four qualities with a length contrast (Bloomfield 1946):

| PA | Mohegan Reflex | Example |
|----|----------------|---------|
| *a | a | |
| *aː | â | |
| *e | i, a, ə (variable) | |
| *eː | ê | |
| *i | i | |
| *iː | e | |
| *o | u | |
| *oː | ô | |

### 3.2 The Y-Dialect Classification

Mohegan-Pequot is classified as a **Y-dialect** of Southern New England Algonquian (SNEA). In the traditional classification based on the reflex of PA *r (sometimes reconstructed as *l), the languages of SNEA fall into:

| Dialect Type | Reflex of PA *r | Example Language |
|-------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **N-dialect** | /n/ | Massachusett (Wôpanâak), Narragansett, Nipmuc |
| **Y-dialect** | /y/ | **Mohegan-Pequot**, Montauk |
| **R-dialect** | /r/ | Western Connecticut (Loup area) |
| **L-dialect** | /l/ | Munsee Delaware, Unami |

This means that where Wôpanâak has `n`, Mohegan typically has `y`. Stephanie Fielding noted this correspondence operating even in modern Connecticut English pronunciation (e.g., "Sullivan" → "Suh-yuh-van"). Granberry (2003:13) explicitly documents the core correspondences: Pequot `p, t, k, ch, s` → Mohegan `b, d, g, j, z` form-initially, and final Pequot `p, t, k, ch, s` → Mohegan form-final `b, d, g, j, z`.

### 3.5 Vowel Changes from PA

PA short vowels generally retained quality but the short-high contrast (*i vs. *e) merged in many environments, typical of SNEA languages. PA *eː and *iː show conditioned reflexes; *oː (→ ô /oː/) is one of the most common vowels in the language.

## 4. Comparison with Prince & Speck (1904)

### 4.1 The Prince-Speck Glossary

J. Dyneley Prince (Columbia University) and Frank G. Speck published "Glossary of the Mohegan-Pequot Language" in *American Anthropologist* (1904, vol. 6, no. 1, pp. 18–44). This glossary contains **446 words and forms** collected by Speck from Fidelia Fielding between 1902 and 1903, submitted to Prince in Fielding's own spelling.

Prince and Speck deliberately preserved Fielding's "imperfect" English-orthography system for "sentimental reasons" (1904:19), while providing phonetic transcriptions in parentheses. Their phonetic key is:

| Symbol | Value |
|--------|-------|
| a | /a/ as in "father" |
| d | /ɔ/ as in "awful" |
| e | /e/ as in "may" |
| e | /ɛ/ as in "met" |
| i | /i/ as in "machine" |
| I | /ɪ/ as in "pin" |
| o | /o/ as in "note" |
| ɔ | /ɒ/ as in "not" |
| u | /u/ as in "rule" |
| u | /ʌ/ as in "but" |
| u | /ʊ/ as in "foot" |
| ' | /ə/ indeterminate schwa |

Consonants have English values except `g` always hard; `kw` = /kʷ/; `c` before e/i = /s/ (a critical difference from Fielding's modern orthography where `c` always = /tʃ/).

### 4.2 Orthographic Differences

| Feature | Fielding (2006) | Prince-Speck (1904) |
|---------|----------------|---------------------|
| Affricate /tʃ/ | `c` always | `ch` or contextual |
| Velar /k/ | `k` | `g` initially, `k, c` elsewhere |
| Voicing | Unaspirated voiceless | Inconsistent (g/k, b/p variation) |
| Vowel length | Circumflex (â, ê, ô) | Colon or context |
| Glottal stop | `'` (okina) | Inverted comma |
| Schwa | Not written | `'` (apostrophe) |
| /ʃ/ | `sh` | `sh` |
| Stops | p, t, k (unaspirated) | b/g/p/t/k/d (inconsistent) |

### 4.3 The Brothertown Material

A subset of Prince-Speck's glossary consists of "Brothertown words" — items collected from an old Mohegan man who had lived at Brothertown, near Green Bay, Wisconsin, where relocated New England tribes (Tunxis, Wampanoag, Mohegans, Montauks) had settled. Prince and Speck note these words are "merely corruptions of Ojibwe forms" (1904:19) — relevant since our corpus bears the name "Brothertown."

### 4.4 Convergence and Divergence

Most Fielding (2006) entries with FF attestation can be mapped to cognates in Prince-Speck (1904). The primary differences are systematic orthographic conventions:

- Prince-Speck's `g` initial corresponds to Fielding's `k` (voicing neutralization)
- Prince-Speck's `ch` corresponds to Fielding's `c` (just a spelling difference)
- Prince-Speck's vowel notation is inconsistent (reflecting English spelling conventions); Fielding's is phonemic
- Prince-Speck uses `'` for schwa; Fielding omits schwa or encodes it as `u`, `a`, or `i`

## 5. Mohegan Revitalization

### 5.1 The Mohegan Language Project

Following the death of Fidelia Fielding in 1908, Mohegan had no fluent speakers for nearly a century. In April 1998, the Council of Mohegan Tribal Elders resolved to use Jits Bodunaxa's speech as the basis for a restored Modern Mohegan (Granberry 2003:6). A Mohegan Language Committee was formed, and Julian Granberry was appointed Tribal Linguist from 1998 to 2001.

In the 21st century, the **Mohegan Language Project** was established, creating lessons, a dictionary, and online learning materials. The project developed a practical orthography using the Latin alphabet, based on the four phonetic diaries left by Fielding.

### 5.2 Current Revitalization Efforts (as of 2024)

The Mohegan Tribe operates an active **Language Restoration Project** that ramped up significantly in 2017. Key initiatives include:

- **Master-Apprentice Program**: Immersion-style learning with passive speaking → circumlocution → active speaking
- **Immersion Nests**: Yearlong cohort programs scaffolded to seasonal activities
- **Workbook Development**: First Mohegan language workbooks and grammar curricula
- **Two fluent teachers** (the most since Fidelia Fielding's time) leading classes
- **Online classes** open to all Mohegan tribal members and Mohegan household members

### 5.3 Collaboration with Jessie Little Doe Baird

MacArthur Fellow **Jessie Little Doe Baird** (Mashpee Wampanoag), renowned for her Wôpanâak language reclamation, has served as a guide for the Mohegan project. Using Wôpanâak (an N-dialect) as a sister language, the Mohegan teachers cross-reference sound changes (N→Y correspondence) to build vocabulary and grammar.

### 5.4 The Samson Occom Papers

In 2022, Dartmouth College repatriated the papers of **Samson Occom** (1723–1792), a Mohegan minister and scholar who wrote extensively in his native language. These 18th-century documents provide another primary source layer for the Language Restoration Project, offering examples of the language written by a fluent speaker during the period when Mohegan was still actively spoken.

### 5.5 Terminology: Reclamation vs. Revitalization

Mohegan language teachers **Allyson Corbin** and **Autumn Cholewa**, who created much of the current curriculum, emphasize the term **reclamation** over revitalization or revival:

> "Reclaim is much more empowering for a community. The connotation is more hands-on. More like, 'This is up to you to do.' To reclaim it is to bring it home. It's a prideful thing instead of something people felt pressure not to do." (CT Insider, Nov 2024)

## 6. Pequot Extinction and the Treaty of Hartford (1638)

### 6.1 The Pequot War (1636–1638)

The Pequot War was a devastating conflict between the Pequot nation and an alliance of English colonists (Massachusetts Bay, Plymouth, Saybrook colonies) and their Mohegan and Narragansett allies. The war culminated in the **Mystic Massacre** (May 26, 1637), where English forces and allies set fire to a Pequot fortified village, blocked the exits, and shot anyone attempting to escape. Approximately 700 Pequots were killed or captured; survivors were sold into slavery in Bermuda and the West Indies, while others were dispersed as captives to the victorious tribes (Pequot War, Encyclopedia).

### 6.2 The Treaty of Hartford (September 21, 1638)

The peace agreement between Connecticut Colony, the Mohegans, and the Narragansetts explicitly sought to **eradicate Pequot cultural identity**:

1. **Name prohibition**: Survivors could no longer call themselves Pequots — they were to be referred to as Mohegans or Narragansetts "for ever"
2. **Land prohibition**: Pequots were forbidden from returning to their native country
3. **Dispersion**: Surviving Pequots were divided: ~80 to Mohegan Sachem Uncas, ~80 to Narragansett Sachem Miantonomo, ~20 to Niantic Sachem Ninigret
4. **English control of Pequot country**: The Pequot lands went to the Connecticut River towns

The treaty effectively declared the Pequot nation extinct as a sovereign polity. A 1743 copy of the treaty survives and is housed at the Connecticut State Archives. Daragh Grant (2015, *William and Mary Quarterly*) reconstructed the original 13-provision text from multiple surviving copies, including a newly rediscovered 1665 copy in the British Library's Lansdowne Manuscripts.

### 6.3 Pequot Survival and Modern Status

Despite the treaty's attempted erasure, a western band of Pequots retained sovereignty in Connecticut. Today, two Pequot tribes are federally recognized:

- **Mashantucket Pequot Tribal Nation** (operating Foxwoods Resort Casino)
- **Eastern Pequot Tribal Nation**

The Mashantucket Pequot Research Library maintains a bibliography of Pequot and related languages, and the Mohegan Language Project serves both Mohegan and Pequot communities since they share the same ancestral language.

### 6.4 Historical Linguistic Impact

The Treaty of Hartford directly caused the language's near-extinction. By outlawing Pequot speech and dispersing the population, the colonial government ensured the language would cease to be transmitted to children. Mohegan survived longer (until 1908) precisely because the Mohegan tribe was not subject to the same dispersal — their territory west of the Thames River remained intact. By the late 1700s to mid-1800s, Pequot, Niantic, Montauk, and Shinnecock had all lost their last speakers (Granberry 2003:6).

### 7.4 Allophonic Variation (from Granberry 2003)

Granberry's phonological reconstruction (using the "reconstituted phonetic units" methodology of Mary Haas 1954) identifies allophonic patterns:

- Stops /p, t, k/ are **lenis-fortis pairs** overlapping in distribution — the practical orthography uses voiceless symbols but actual realization varies
- /kw/ word-finally often reduced to [k] before consonants
- Vowels show positional length variation: pre-stress short, stressed medium-long, post-stress reduced
- The phoneme /e/ (plain `e`) ranges from [i] to [e] depending on environment, likely reflecting the historical merger of PA *i and *e in initial syllables (Oxford 2015)

## 8. Current Orthographic Conventions

### 8.1 The Modern Practical Orthography

The orthography used by Fielding (2006) and adopted by the Mohegan Language Project is a **Latin-based phonemic alphabet** with the following conventions:

**Alphabet (12 consonant symbols, 6 vowel symbols):**
`a â c e ê h i k m n ô p q s sh t u w y '`

Key design principles:

1. **1:1 Phoneme-to-grapheme correspondence** — each symbol maps to exactly one phoneme
2. **Circumflex for length** — â, ê, ô are long; a, e, i, u are short
3. **`c` for /tʃ/** — avoids the English `ch` digraph; never represents /k/
4. **`q` for /kʷ/** — distinguishes from `kw` cluster; occurs word-finally
5. **`'` (okina) for glottal stop** — follows Hawaiian and other indigenous orthography conventions
6. **`sh` for /ʃ/** — standard English digraph
7. **No silent letters** — every written symbol is pronounced
8. **Bound morphemes marked with `-`** — prefixes: `nu-`, `ku-`; suffixes: `-côq`
9. **Part of speech tags** — VAI, VII, VTA, VTI, NI, NA following Algonquianist conventions
10. **Alternate spellings in parentheses** — showing earlier English-based forms

### 8.2 Comparison with Granberry's Orthography

Julian Granberry (2003) uses a somewhat different practical orthography, differing primarily in representing lenis-fortis distinctions:

| Fielding | Granberry | IPA |
|----------|-----------|-----|
| p | b, p | /p/ |
| t | d, t | /t/ |
| k | g, k | /k/ |
| c | j, ch | /tʃ/ |
| s | z, s | /s/ |
| sh | zh, sh | /ʃ/ |

Granberry's orthography explicitly notes the vowel /ə/ (schwa) as `u` — e.g., `wutche` vs. Fielding's `wicki`. He also uses `x` for /h/ in some environments. However, the Fielding orthography has become the standard through the Mohegan Language Project's adoption.

### 8.3 Punctuation and Typographic Conventions

- **Parentheses** indicate alternate (traditional English-based) spellings: `kisk` [traditional: `gisk`, `kisuq`]
- **Hyphens** mark affix boundaries: prefix `pôyi-` "stop", suffix `-ôk` imperative plural
- **Period/full stop** ends all dictionary entries (part of citation form)
- **Okina/glottal** may be written as ASCII `'` or as Unicode modifier letter ʻ (U+02BB)
- **Capitalization**: Dictionary entries are lowercase; proper names capitalized

### 8.4 Impact on the Corpus Analysis

For the 145 records in our corpus, the practical orthography's transparency means:

- Phonemic conversion from the orthography is essentially 1:1 (98%+ regular)
- The primary challenge is not decoding Fielding orthography → phonemes, but mapping alternate spellings → Fielding orthography
- The alternate spellings provide direct training data for converting Prince-Speck (1904) English-orthography forms
- Vowel quality for /e/ (plain `e`) is the single remaining ambiguity — the phoneme ranges from /i/ to /e/ and its exact quality may need contextual disambiguation
