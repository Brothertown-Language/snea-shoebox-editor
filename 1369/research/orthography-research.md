# Edwards 1787 Mahican Orthography — Expanded Research Card

## Summary

Jonathan Edwards Jr.'s 1788 publication *Observations on the Language of the
Muhhekaneew Indians* is the earliest substantial grammatical description of
Mahican and one of the most important documents for comparative Algonquian
linguistics in the 18th century. This card compiles what is known about the
orthography he used, the sources for interpreting it, and the phonological
system underlying his transcriptions.

---

## 1. Jonathan Edwards Jr. — Biographical Context

| Detail | Information |
|--------|-------------|
| Full name | Jonathan Edwards (the younger) |
| Life | May 26, 1745 – August 1, 1801 |
| Father | Jonathan Edwards Sr. (theologian, Princeton's 3rd president) |
| Education | Princeton University, graduated 1765 |
| Occupations | Congregationalist minister, theologian, college president, linguist |
| Presidency | Union College, Schenectady NY, 1799–1801 (died in office) |
| Key date | 1787/1788 — published his Mahican study |

Edwards grew up in Stockbridge, Massachusetts, a "praying town" where Mahican
people were the majority population (≈150 Mahican families versus ≈12 white
families). He learned Mahican as a child from playmates, becoming fluent. At
age 10 (1755), his father sent him to live in the Iroquois settlement of
Onaquaga (Onohoquaga) to learn Oneida, giving him first-hand knowledge of
both Algonquian and Iroquoian language families.

Unlike his father, who refused to learn Mahican (calling it "barbarous"),
Edwards Jr. defended the language's complexity, demonstrated that it had
abstract terms (love, hatred, malice, religion), and translated the Lord's
Prayer into Mahican. He consulted a Mahican elder, "Captain Yohum," for
revisions before publication. George Washington, James Madison, and Thomas
Jefferson all received copies of his treatise.

## 2. The 1788 Publication

**Full title:**

> *Observations on the Language of the Muhhekaneew Indians; in which the
> Extent of that Language in North-America is shewn; its Genius
> Grammatically Traced; some of its Peculiarities, and some Instances of
> Analogy between that and the Hebrew are Pointed Out.*

Communicated to the Connecticut Society of Arts and Sciences on October 23,
1787. Published 1788 by Josiah Meigs, New Haven. A second edition with notes
by John Pickering appeared in 1823 (in Massachusetts Historical Collections).

The treatise includes:
- A grammatical sketch of Mahican (parts of speech, person marking, tense)
- A comparative glossary: English–Mahican–Shawnee (p. 6)
- A comparative glossary: English–Mahican–Chippeway/Ojibwa (pp. 7–8)
- Numerals 1–10 in Mahican and Mohawk (p. 9)
- The Lord's Prayer in Mahican and in the "language of the Six Nations"
- Word lists organized by semantic domain
- Observations on the geographic extent of Algonquian languages
- Speculative comparison with Hebrew (a common 18th-c. trope)

Edwards uses the term "Mohegan" interchangeably with "Muhhekaneew" for the
language, though he distinguishes it clearly from Mohegan-Pequot (Southern
New England). His dialect is specifically Stockbridge Mahican.

The full text is available online:
- Archive.org: https://archive.org/details/observationsonla01edwa
- MPG Pure: https://pure.mpg.de/rest/items/item_1909633_6/component/file_1921111/content
- Canadiana: https://www.canadiana.ca/view/oocihm.47086

## 3. Edwards' Orthographic System

Edwards uses an English-based ad-hoc orthography typical of 18th-century
colonial sources. He provides a pronunciation key indicating his intended
sound values for vowel letters.

### 3.1 Consonant Values (Relatively Clear)

| Grapheme | Likely value | Evidence |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| `p, t, k` | /p, t, k/ (unaspirated or lightly aspirated) | PA *p, *t, *k > Mahican /p, t, k/ |
| `b, d, g` | Probably /p, t, k/ after nasals or in voiced contexts; may reflect allophonic voicing | Unclear; English-orthography convention |
| `m, n` | /m, n/ | Straightforward |
| `s` | /s/ | PA *s, *š merged to /s/ in Mahican by modern period |
| `sh` | /š/ | English convention |
| `h` | /h/ | Explicitly written; Edwards is unusually consistent here |
| `w, y` | /w, j/ | Glides |
| `ch` | /č/ | English convention |
| `gh` | **UNKNOWN** — see §5.1 | Candidatally /x/ or /h/ |

### 3.2 Vowel Values (More Ambiguous)

The key insight (Goddard 2008, Masthay 1991, Warne) is that Edwards uses `e`
to represent /iː/ in many cases. This is confirmed by his pronunciation key
and by cognate comparison.

| Edwards grapheme | Likely Mahican phoneme | PA source | Example |
|------------------|----------------------|-----------|---------|
| `ee` | /iː/ | PA *iː | `neesoh` 'two' (PA *niːšwi) |
| `e` | /iː/ or /i/ | PA *i(ː) | `seepoo` 'river' (PA *siːpoːwi) |
| `a` | /a/ | PA *a, *eː → PEA *aː | `nannah` 'five' (PA *nyaːlanwi) |
| `o, oo` | /o/ or /u/ | PA *o(ː) | — |
| `u` | /ə/ (schwa) in unstressed; possibly /o/ elsewhere | PA *e → PEA *ə | `muhhekaneew` (with məh-) |
| `au, aw` | /a/ + /w/ or /aː/ | — | — |
| `uh` | /əh/ or /ə/ | — | Frequent in final syllables |

### 3.3 Key Phonological Correspondences (PA → Mahican)

From Warne's analysis of the Schmick manuscript, confirmed across sources:

| PA | Old Mahican | Modern Mahican | Notes |
|----|-------------|----------------|-------|
| *p | /p/ | /p/ | No change |
| *t | /t/ | /t/ | No change |
| *k | /k/ | /k/ | No change |
| *č | /č/ | /č/ | No change |
| *θ | /n/ | /n/ | **Merged** with *n by Old Mahican period |
| *l | /n/ | /n/ | **Merged** with *n by Old Mahican period |
| *n | /n/ | /n/ | — |
| *s | /s/ | /s/ | — |
| *š | /s/ | /s/ | Merged to /s/ by Modern Mahican |
| *m | /m/ | /m/ | — |
| *h | /h/ | /h/ | — |
| *iː | /iː/ | /iː/ | — |
| *i | /i/ | /i/ | — |

| *e | /ə/ (schwa) | /ə/ | Reduced to schwa (pan-EA development) |

| *oː | /uː/ | /uː/ | — |

### 3.4 The /iː/ vs. /eː/ Issue

Recent work (documented in *ImportantMahicanVowel2.pdf* from
munseedelaware.com, with consultation by Goddard and Masthay) has established
that Edwards' `e` frequently represents /iː/, NOT /eː/. Goddard confirmed
(c. 2023 email, cited in the Mahican vowel paper) that:

> The second vowel of our tribal name was absolutely not /eː/ and is properly
> the "lower high" version of the vowel /iː/. This vowel is produced by
> setting the throat and tongue in a position to say the English word "hit"
> and then producing the sound.

Evidence:
- Edwards writes `Muhhekaneew` — the second vowel is /iː/
- `Neesoh` 'two' — cf. PA *niːšwi, Munsee /niːšə/
- `Seepoo` 'river' — cf. PA *siːpoːwi, Munsee /siːpəw/
- Goddard's 2008 paper transcribes Dick's /kīsōx/ 'sun' and /θīpəw/ 'river'
  with /iː/ — even where the English spelling suggests "e."
- Goddard's 2010 phoneme inventory for Mahican lists NO /eː/ phoneme.

**Impact on orthography conversion:** Edwards' `e` should NOT be mapped to
/e/ or /ɛ/ by default. The default mapping should be /i(ː)/ unless cognate
evidence specifically indicates a different value.

## 4. Comparative Sources for Orthography Interpretation

### 4.1 The Schmick Manuscript (Primary Internal Source)

| Detail | Information |
|--------|-------------|
| Author | Johann Jacob Schmick, Moravian missionary |
| Period | c. 1753–1767 (before Edwards) |
| Dialect | Moravian/Western Mahican (Ohio/PA branch) |
| Format | Two volumes (322 pp.), German-based orthography |
| Publication | Masthay (ed.), *Schmick's Mahican Dictionary*, APS Memoir 197 (1991) |
| Contains | Mahican historical phonology by David Pentland |

Schmick's manuscript uses a German-based orthography which is in some ways
more precise than Edwards' English-based system:
- German `ch` for /x/ (vs. Edwards' ambiguous `gh`)
- More consistent vowel representation
- Extensive vocabulary (much larger than Edwards' 144 records)

However, Schmick shows inconsistency with vowels — especially the reflex of
PA *aː which he writes variously as `a, aa, ã, ã, a` (Warne). The tilde
likely indicates a secondary nasalization feature.

**Key finding:** Schmick is much more consistent with consonants than vowels.
Identical forms appear written with different vowel symbols or sequences.
(Warne, *Time-Depth in Mahican Diachronic Phonology*)

**Pentland's historical phonology** (in Masthay 1991) provides the connecting
framework between PA reconstructions and Mahican forms. **Goddard (2007–2008)**
provided important corrections to Pentland's phonology, particularly in the
treatment of vowel length and the PA *e reflexes.

### 4.2 Zeisberger's Delaware Materials

David Zeisberger (1721–1808), Moravian missionary, produced extensive
documentation of Munsee Delaware (a close relative of Mahican within the
Delawaran subgroup):
- *Zeisberger's Delaware Vocabulary of 1772* (earliest extensive Munsee
  vocabulary)
- *Grammar of the Language of the Lenni Lenape or Delaware Indians* (1827)
- *Diary of David Zeisberger* (several volumes)

These are important for comparative cognate work because Munsee and Mahican
share a common ancestor (Delawaran, per Goddard) and often show parallel
developments.

### 4.3 Later Mahican Sources

| Source | Period | Dialect | Content |
|--------|--------|---------|---------|
| Hendrick Aupaumut writings | c. 1790s–1820s | Stockbridge Mahican | Letters, documents by a fluent Mahican speaker |
| Wm. Dick (Michelson notes) | c. 1910s | Modern Mahican (Wisconsin) | Phonetic transcriptions |
| Bernice Robinson recordings | mid-20th c. | Modern Mahican | Audio recordings of the last speakers |

### 4.4 Secondary Literature

## 5. Phonological Processes Affecting Edwards' Orthography

### 5.1 The `gh` Grapheme — Key Ambiguity

Edwards uses `gh` in several words where the phonological value is uncertain.
Cross-linguistic comparison suggests multiple possible values:

| Candidate | Evidence For | Evidence Against |
|-----------|-------------|------------------|
| /x/ (voiceless velar fricative) | Schmick uses German `ch` for /x/ in cognate forms; PA *h before consonant sometimes appears as /x/ in daughter languages | — |
| /h/ | Simple /h/ is the default reflex of PA *h | Edwards also writes `h` separately, so `gh` may be a distinct phoneme |
| /k/ (lenis/voiced allophone) | English `gh` occasionally used for /k/ in colonial sources | Unlikely given that Edwards has `k` for /k/ |

**Recommended resolution:** Use comparative evidence. If a cognate from
Schmick, Munsee, or Wampanoag shows /x/, the mapping is /x/. If it shows /h/,
the mapping is /h/. If no cognate exists, flag as ambiguous.

### 5.2 Vowel Reduction and `uh`

Edwards frequently writes `u` and `uh` in unstressed syllables. The PA *e
regularly became schwa /ə/ in Proto-Eastern Algonquian. In Edwards'
orthography:
- Unstressed `u` most likely = /ə/
- `uh` may represent /əh/ or breathy/schwa in a checked syllable
- Munsee shows parallel reduction: PA *e → PEA *ə → Munsee /ə/
  (raised to /i/ or /u/ before certain clusters)

### 5.3 Nasalization of *aː

PA *aː developed a nasal quality in Mahican, marked by Schmick with a tilde
(ã). Warne considers this a "secondary feature" that was optional in Old
Mahican but generalized to ALL instances of /aː/ by the Modern Mahican
period. Edwards does NOT mark nasalization, so Edwards' `a` may represent
either oral /aː/ or nasal /ãː/ depending on cognate evidence.

### 5.4 Word-Final Vowel Deletion

PA word-final vowels were deleted in Mahican (a pan-Eastern Algonquian
process). Edwards sometimes writes these (reflecting an older/more careful
pronunciation) and sometimes omits them. Example:
- PA *-i (plural marker) → sometimes written as `-eh` or `-uh` in
  Edwards, sometimes absent.

### 5.5 Schwa Lowering Before /h, x/

Before /h/ and /x/, schwa /ə/ is lowered to /a/ in Mahican (Warne). This
accounts for alternations where Edwards writes `a` where PA reconstruction
predicts *e. Example: `ntah` 'my heart' < PA *-teːh- (with *eː → *a and
schwa-lowering in certain environments).

### 5.6 Rounding Assimilation

PA *e rounds to Mahican /o/ when adjacent to *w. This explains forms like
the word for 'tree' and 'beaver' where Edwards' `o` corresponds to PA *e.
(Wane's rule ordering: rounding before laxing.)

## 6. Morphological Patterns in Edwards' Data

Edwards' data reveals several regular morphological patterns that can aid
word segmentation before orthography conversion:

### 6.1 Person Prefixes

| Person | Prefix | Edwards example | Gloss |
|--------|--------|-----------------|-------|
| 1st sg. | n- | `ncmeetseh, ncmeetfeh` | 'I eat' |
| 2nd sg. | k- | `kcmeetseh, ka meetjeh` | 'you eat' |
| 3rd sg. | w-/∅- | `pumiffoo` | 'he walks' |

Edwards himself noted these correspondences and compared them to Hebrew
prefixes, which was one of his major observations.

### 6.2 Verbal Suffixes

Edwards records frequent verbal suffixes:
- `-uh, -eh, -seh` — likely aspect/mood markers
- `-oo` — 3rd person singular independent indicative (e.g., `pumiffoo`)
- `-nuh` — 1st person plural (e.g., `ncmeetfchnuh` 'we eat')

### 6.3 Noun Inflection

- Possessive prefixes precede nouns (e.g., `noghkuh` 'my father')
- Plural formation via suffixation (e.g., `penumpaufoo` 'boy' →
  `penumpaufoouk` 'boys')

## 7. Known Ambiguities in Edwards' Orthography

1. **`gh` grapheme** — Unknown value (/x/, /h/, or other). Must be resolved
   per cognate on a case-by-case basis. This is the single biggest ambiguity.

2. **`uh` sequences** — May represent /ə/, /əh/, or /uh/ depending on
   context. Most unstressed `u` = /ə/, but checked syllables may differ.

3. **Vowel quality** — Edwards' English-based vowel letters are ambiguous.
   The /iː/ = `e` mapping (Goddard) is the most important correction.
   `a` could be /a/, /aː/, or /ãː/.

4. **Word segmentation** — Multi-word entries in the sentence-level data
   need morphological parsing before phoneme conversion.

5. **Word-initial clusters** — Need morphological analysis to determine
   whether initial CC sequences are prefix+root or root-internal.

6. **No phonetic transcriptions** — Edwards did not use a systematic phonetic
   alphabet. No `\ph` entries exist.

7. **Small corpus** — ≈144 records limits statistical approaches.

8. **Dialect differences** — Stockbridge Mahican (Edwards) vs. Moravian
   Mahican (Schmick) may have phonological differences.

9. **18th-century conventions** — Edwards' spellings reflect his own English
   pronunciation as much as Mahican sounds; e.g., `oo` may be /uː/ or /oː/.

## 8. Recommended Approach

### 8.1 DO NOT USE REGEX-ONLY

Sentence-level parsing, `gh` ambiguity, word-initial cluster
disambiguation, and `uh` vowel reduction all require context-sensitive or
comparative-evidence-based handling.

### 8.2 Multi-Stage Pipeline

**Stage 1: Preprocessing**
- Split multi-word entries into individual tokens
- Flag entries with `gh` for comparative cognate resolution
- Normalize Edwards' typographic conventions (long s, etc.)

**Stage 2: Phoneme Mapping with Cognate-Based Ambiguity Resolution**

Use the following default mapping table, with overrides per cognate:

| Edwards | Default phoneme | Condition | Cognate override source |
|---------|----------------|-----------|------------------------|
| ee | /iː/ | always | — |
| e | /i(ː)/ | default (Goddard) | Exception: check if PA *eː → /a/ |
| a | /a/ or /aː/ | context-dependent | Check PA length; check Schmick for nasalization |
| o, oo | /o/ or /uː/ | context-dependent | Cognate from Munsee or PA |
| u (unstressed) | /ə/ | default | — |
| u (stressed) | /o/ or /u/ | likely | Cognate-dependent |
| uh | /əh/ | usually | — |
| p, t, k | /p, t, k/ | always | — |
| b, d, g | /p, t, k/ | after nasals? | Flag for review |
| gh | **FLAG** | always | Resolve per cognate (Schmick /x/ or /h/) |
| s | /s/ | always | — |
| sh | /š/ | always | — |
| ch | /č/ | always | — |
| h | /h/ | always | Edwards is reliable for /h/ |
| w, y | /w, j/ | always | — |
| m, n | /m, n/ | always | — |

**Stage 3: Morphological Segmentation**
- Identify and strip person prefixes (n-, k-, w-)
- Segment verbal suffixes (-uh, -eh, -seh, -oo, -nuh)
- Produce root forms for lexicon building

**Stage 4: Cross-Validation**
- Compare converted forms against Schmick's phonemicized forms
- Compare against Munsee cognates (Zeisberger, Goddard)
- Compare against PA reconstructions (Pentland, Siebert)
- Flag entries where converted phonemes mismatch expected reflexes

### 8.4 Software Architecture

Given the ambiguities and the need for comparative-evidence-based resolution,
the conversion system should be implemented as:

- **FSM core** for the unambiguous mappings (consonants, clear vowels)
- **Cognate database** providing priors for ambiguous mappings (`gh`, `e`
  values, vowel length)
- **Resolver module** that queries the cognate database before applying
  ambiguous rules; falls back to a default with a confidence flag if no
  cognate found
- **Output format**: phonemic transcription with confidence markers:
  - `[high]` = confirmed by cognate
  - `[med]` = default rule, no contradicting cognate
  - `[low]` = guess; no cognate available
  - `[FLAG]` = contradictory evidence; manual review needed

---

## 9. Key Priorities for Conversion

1. **Resolve `gh` first** — This is the highest-impact ambiguity. Collect
   all Edwards entries with `gh`, find cognates in Schmick (German `ch`) or
   Munsee, and apply the correct mapping.

2. **Establish the `e` = /iː/ default** — Goddard's finding that Edwards' `e`
   is /iː/ (not /eː/ or /ɛ/) must be the default rule.

3. **Segment before converting** — Person prefixes and verbal suffixes must
   be identified first to avoid misanalyzing prefix vowels as root vowels.

4. **Validate against Schmick forms** — Where Schmick has the same entry in
   a German-based orthography, his forms are the most reliable anchor.

5. **Flag, don't guess** — Where no cognate or comparative evidence exists,
   produce a `[low]` confidence output rather than a potentially incorrect
   conversion.
