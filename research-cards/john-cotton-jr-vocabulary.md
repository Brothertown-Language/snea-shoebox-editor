# Research Card: John Cotton Jr. — Indian Vocabulary (1666–1678)

## 1. Primary Source

**Author:** John Cotton Jr. (1639–1699) — Puritan minister, Harvard graduate (1657), missionary to the Wampanoag on Martha's Vineyard and Cape Cod. Grandson of John Cotton the famous Puritan divine. Served as minister at Martha's Vineyard (1666–1667) and later at Plymouth.

**Manuscript:** Diary and Indian vocabulary, kept from 6 March 1666 to November 1667, and 12 October 1670 to 23 July 1678. The vocabulary is a compendium of words and phrases spoken by the Wampanoag who lived on Martha's Vineyard during the late 17th century.

**Location:** Massachusetts Historical Society, Boston (Ms. N-1042).

**Format:** Small notebook containing a diary (missionary journal) and a Massachuset Indian language vocabulary. The vocabulary was gathered during the first two years of his mission to Native communities on Cape Cod and Martha's Vineyard.

## 2. Dialect Affiliation

Martha's Vineyard Wampanoag — a distinct dialectal variant from the mainland Massachusett (Natick) dialect documented by John Eliot. Key differences from mainland Massachusett are expected in phonology and lexicon, though systematic analysis is limited. The MV Wampanoag dialect is the same variety that the WLRP (Wôpanâak Language Reclamation Project) primarily draws upon for its modern reconstruction.

## 3. Scholarly Treatment

- **Travers, Len (ed.)** — "The Missionary Journal of John Cotton, Jr., 1666–1678" — published in *Proceedings of the Massachusetts Historical Society*, 3rd ser., Vol. 109 (1997), pp. 52–101. This is the definitive modern edition of the diary portion, with annotations. Available via JSTOR.

- **Wôpanâak Inscribed** — An NHPRC-funded project (National Historical Publications and Records Commission) to create a digital edition of the Cotton vocabulary. Status: cataloged at the U.S. National Archives as an active project. URL: https://www.archives.gov/nhprc/projects/catalog/wopanaak-vocabulary

- **Goddard & Bragdon (1988)** — *Native Writings in Massachusett* — includes analysis of MV Wampanoag materials and provides comparative context for Cotton's orthography.

## 4. Estimated Entry Count

The vocabulary portion of the manuscript has not been fully published in a modern edition. The Travers edition focuses on the diary. The "Wôpanâak Inscribed" project aims to produce a digital edition of the vocabulary itself. Based on the manuscript description, it is a substantial lexicon comparable in scope to Josiah Cotton's later vocabulary.

## 5. Orthographic System

Cotton's orthography is ad-hoc English-based, influenced by:
- Early Modern English spelling conventions of the 1660s
- Possible influence from John Eliot's standardized orthography (Cotton was active on MV just as Eliot's Bible translation was being published)
- His own hearing biases as an English speaker learning Wampanoag as a second language

The orthography is less standardized than Eliot's but more systematic than Winslow's or Wood's ad-hoc systems.

## 6. Accessibility Status

- **Manuscript:** Available at Massachusetts Historical Society (Boston). Digital viewer available via MHS Collections Online.
- **Diary transcription:** Published by Travers (1997) in MHS Proceedings — available via JSTOR.
- **Vocabulary digital edition:** "Wôpanâak Inscribed" project in progress (NHPRC-funded). Not yet publicly available as of 2026.
- **Full manuscript images:** Available through MHS online database.

## 8. Key Features

- Represents a **separate dialectal tradition** from Eliot's Natick Massachusett
- The diary provides **sociolinguistic context** for the vocabulary (who said what, when, and in what situation)
- Includes both **everyday vocabulary** and **Christian religious terminology** (Cotton was a missionary)
- The "Wôpanâak Inscribed" digital edition, when completed, will be the first modern publication of the full vocabulary

## 9. Research Gaps

- No complete published transcription of the vocabulary exists (only the diary has been published)
- The "Wôpanâak Inscribed" project status is unclear — listed as an NHPRC project but completion date unknown
- Systematic comparison with Eliot's Massachusett and the mainland Wampanoag sources has not been done
- The relationship between Cotton's orthography and Eliot's standardized system needs analysis
