# Research Card: Winslow's "Good News from New England" (1624) — Wampanoag Vocabulary

## 1. Primary Source & Full Text

Edward Winslow's **"Good Newes from New England: or a true Relation of things very remarkable at the Plantation of Plimoth in New-England"** was published in London in 1624 (printed by I.D. for William Bladen and John Bellamie). The 1841 reprint edited by Alexander Young (in *Chronicles of the Pilgrim Fathers*) is the standard modern source and the basis for the Project Gutenberg text (#66332).

**Available full-text sources:**
- Project Gutenberg (1841 reprint): https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/66332
- Internet Archive (1624 original, John Adams Library copy): https://archive.org/details/goodnewesfromnew00wins
- Modernized version (Caleb Johnson / histarch): http://www.histarch.illinois.edu/plymouth/goodnews0.html
- National Humanities Center PDF: http://nationalhumanitiescenter.org/pds/amerbegin/settlement/text3/GoodNewsNewYork.pdf
- Scholarly edition (Kelly Wisecup, UMass Press 2014): https://www.umasspress.com/9781625340832/

Winslow's account covers events from December 1621 to September 1623, including Massasoit's sickness, Tisquantum's death, Thomas Weston's Wessagussett Colony, and the Pilgrims' conflicts with the Massachusett and Narragansett.

## 2. Author: Edward Winslow (1595–1655)

Mayflower passenger, Plymouth Colony governor (3 terms), primary Pilgrim negotiator with New England Algonquians (Wampanoag, Massachusett, Narragansett). Credited with curing Massasoit of a life-threatening illness in 1623. Served as the Pilgrims' chief agent in England. His work represents one of the earliest English records of Wampanoag vocabulary — predating Roger Williams' *A Key into the Language of America* (1643) and John Eliot's Massachusett Bible (1663).

Winslow himself acknowledged the limitations of his linguistic knowledge, describing the language as "very copious, large, and difficult" and stating that "as yet we cannot attain to any great measure thereof."

## 3. Linguistic Intermediaries

### Tisquantum (Squanto, c. 1585–1622)
Patuxet Wampanoag, kidnapped by Captain Thomas Hunt in 1614, sold as a slave in Málaga, Spain, escaped and lived in London with merchant John Slany. Learned English, returned to Patuxet in 1619 to find his village destroyed by epidemic. Served as interpreter, guide, and advisor to Plymouth Colony from March 1621 until his death in November 1622. Died of "Indian fever" at Monomoit (Chatham, Cape Cod). Winslow describes his "wicked practice" of claiming the English kept the plague buried and could send it forth at will.

### Hobbomock (Hobomok, d. 1642)
Pokanoket pniese (military advisor/champion to Massasoit). Lived among the English, translated, accompanied diplomatic and military missions (including Standish's 1623 raid). Deeply devoted to Massasoit; Winslow records his lament "Neen womasu sagimus" upon hearing of Massasoit's apparent death. Died of European disease.

### Samoset (Abenaki sachem)
First Native American to greet the Pilgrims in English (March 1621) at Plymouth.

## 4. The Wampanoag Word List (22 words/phrases)

According to Claudio Salvucci's analysis in *InteractiveALR Vol. 27: Massachusetts*, there are 22 distinct words and phrases scattered throughout Winslow's text. The following list is compiled from the primary source:

| Word/Phrase | English Translation | Context in Winslow |
|---|---|---|
| **Keen Winsnow?** | "Art thou Winslow?" | Massasoit's greeting to Winslow (l → n substitution noted) |
| **Ahhe** | "Yes" | Winslow's reply to Massasoit |
| **Matta neen wonckanet namen Winsnow** | "O Winslow, I shall never see thee again!" | Massasoit's deathbed lament |
| **Winsnow** | "Winslow" | Illustrates l → n substitution; Wampanoag/Massachusett had no /l/ |
| **Neen womasu sagimus** | "My loving sachem" | Hobbomok's lament for Massasoit |
| **Sachimo comaco** | "The sachem's place" / "The king's house" | Massasoit's dwelling at Pokanoket |
| **Witeo** | "An ordinary house" | Ordinary vs. sachem's dwelling |
| **Squasachim** | "The sachem's wife" | Referring to Corbitant's wife |
| **Maskiet** | "Physic" / "Medicine" | Context of healing |
| **Kiehtan** | (The supreme deity / "the same power that we called God") | Winslow's discussion of religious beliefs |
| **Chise** | "An old man" | Winslow's etymology for Kiehtan |
| **Kiehchise** | "A man that exceedeth in age" | Comparative form |
| **Hinnaim namen, hinnaim michen, matta cuts** | "By and by it should see, and by and by it should eat, but not speak" | Describing a dying person |
| **Quatchet** | "Walk abroad" | |
| **Sachim** | "King" / "Chief" | General term |
| **Pniese** (also **pinse**, **pinese**) | "One of his chiefest champions, or men of valor" | Military advisor class |
| **Powah** | "One of special note; calls upon the devil, cures diseases" | Shaman/priest |
| **Squaw** | "A woman" | Used in compound "squasachim" |

**Note on pidginization:** Goddard (1977) identified that Winslow's data shows "a good deal of pidginization" — the English were likely learning a simplified contact variety rather than the full grammatical language. The most famous example is "Matta neen wonckanet namen Winsnow" — where *namen* ('see it', transitive inanimate) is used instead of the correct transitive animate verb that would be expected for seeing a person.

## 5. Dialect Affiliation

Winslow's data has not been systematically analyzed for dialect affiliation. Based on his location at Plymouth and his contact with the Pokanoket band of the Wampanoag Confederacy (led by Massasoit), the vocabulary is expected to represent the speech of the **Pokanoket** people — a sub-group of the Wampanoag, whose dialect was closely related to the Massachusett language of eastern Massachusetts.

## 6. Cross-Comparisons with Other Early Sources

| Source | Year | Scope | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Williams, *A Key into the Language of America*** | 1643 | 208-page Narragansett guide | Systematic topical vocabulary; far more comprehensive |
| **Wood, *New England's Prospect*** | 1630 | Small dictionary in appendix | Postdates Winslow; similar pidgin features |
| **Sheperd (attrib.), *The Day-Breaking*** | 1647 | 11 scattered words/phrases | Massachusett proper (Neponset/Nonantum area) |
| **Eliot, Massachusett Bible** | 1663 | Full Bible translation | Abundant grammatical data in Natick dialect |

Key comparative observations:
- Winslow's *Kiehtan* corresponds to Williams' *Kautántowwit* (Narragansett great spirit) and Eliot's *Keihtan*
- *Sachim* vs. Abenakian *sagamore* — both from Proto-Algonquian *sa·kima·wa*; Winslow uses *sachim*
- Winslow's *witeo* contrasts with Massachusett *wetu* and the pan-Algonquian *wigwam* that appears in other sources
- The l → n substitution (Winslow → *Winsnow*) is consistent across all early English records

## 7. Orthographic Conventions

Winslow's English orthography was Early Modern English (pre-orthographic standardization). His rendering of Wampanoag words follows English sound-spelling conventions of the 1620s:

| Winslow Spelling | Likely Phonetic Value | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Keen** | /keːn/ | Represents *kīn* 'you' (animate singular) |
| **Winsnow** | /wɪnsnoʊ/ | /l/ → /n/ substitution (Wampanoag had no /l/) |
| **Ahhe** | /ahə/ or /ahɛ/ | 'yes' |
| **Matta** | /mata/ | Negative 'not' — widespread across Algonquian languages |
| **neen** | /neːn/ | 'I' (first person singular independent pronoun) |
| **wonckanet** | /wɒŋkənet/ | 'again' |
| **namen** | /naːmən/ | Transitive inanimate 'see (it)' |
| **womasu** | /womasu/ | 'loving' — warped by English ear? |
| **sagimus** | /sagɪmʊs/ | *sachim* + possessive suffix 'my' |
| **comaco** | /kɒmako/ | *comaco* as 'place/house of' — also appears in "Sachimo comaco" |
| **Kiehtan** | /kiːhtan/ or /kajhtan/ | Supreme deity — "has reference to antiquity" per Winslow |
| **Maskiet** | /maskiːt/ or /maskiɛt/ | 'physic/medicine' |

No standardized transcription system existed. The 1841 Young edition reprints Winslow's text with added footnotes but does not normalize the Native-language spellings.

## 8. Key Linguistic Features

1. **Pidginization** (Goddard 1977, also *Massachusett Pidgin* Wikipedia article): The English contact variety simplified verb forms. In "Matta neen wonckanet namen Winsnow":
   - *namen* is transitive inanimate ('see it') rather than the correct transitive animate form (*nunaw* 'I see him')
   - Pro-drop is lost; pronouns become obligatory since verb inflection is simplified
   - This is clear evidence that both the English and Wampanoag were communicating via a simplified pidgin, not the full grammatical language

2. **Phonological substitution**: English /l/ and /r/ → Wampanoag /n/ (since Massachusett lacked both /l/ and /r/) — evidenced by "Winslow" → *Winsnow* and "Frenchmen" → *panachmonog*.

3. **Lexical borrowing into English**: Winslow's work contains some of the earliest English attestations of squaw (1621, published 1622), sachem/sachim, powah/powwow, and wigwam — all of which entered American English via the Massachusett Pidgin contact variety.

4. **Religious terminology**: Winslow provides valuable early documentation of Wampanoag spiritual concepts — *Kiehtan* (supreme god), *powah* (shaman/healer), *Hobbamock* (a healing spirit/demiurge), all described in the same text.

## Research Gaps

- No systematic modern linguistic analysis of the dialect affiliation of Winslow's Pokanoket vocabulary (flagged by Salvucci)
- The pidginization thesis (Goddard 1977) rests on limited data points; a full reassessment with modern Algonquianist methodology would be valuable
- Comparative analysis with the Sheperd vocabulary (11 words from 1647, Massachusett proper) has barely been attempted
- The relationship between Winslow's *Kiehtan* and the Narragansett *Kautántowwit* (Williams 1643) and Massachusett *Keihtan* (Eliot 1663) needs updated comparative treatment
