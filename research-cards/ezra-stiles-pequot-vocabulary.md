# Research Card: Ezra Stiles — Pequot Vocabulary (1762)

## 1. Primary Source

**Author:** Ezra Stiles (1727–1795) — Congregational clergyman, seventh president of Yale College (1778–1795), one of the founders of Brown University. An avid collector of scientific, historical, and linguistic data. Compiled the Pequot vocabulary during his "Itineraries" — notes from his travels through Connecticut.

**Manuscript:** "A Vocabulary of the Pequot Indians" (1762), contained within Stiles' "Miscellanies" (MVP #200), Ezra Stiles Papers, Series V. Yale University Beinecke Library, OID 2007275, Box 47, Folder 1971.

**Informant:** An unnamed Pequot Indian at Groton, Connecticut. The informant's identity is not recorded in the manuscript.

**Secondary analysis:** William Cowan (1973) — "Pequot from Stiles to Speck" — *International Journal of American Linguistics* 39(3): 164–172. This is the definitive modern analysis, comparing Stiles' 1762 data with Speck's 1904/1928 data to track language change and attrition over 140+ years.

## 2. Dialect Affiliation

Pequot — a dialect of Mohegan-Pequot (Eastern Algonquian, Y-dialect). The Pequot language was spoken in southeastern Connecticut. After the Pequot War (1637), the surviving Pequot were divided into Eastern (Stonington) and Western (Groton) groups. Stiles' informant was from the Groton (Western) Pequot community.

## 3. Estimated Entry Count

~159 forms, with the last ~50 being numbers from 11 to 1000. This is a relatively small vocabulary but valuable as the earliest systematic Pequot documentation.

## 4. Orthographic System

Stiles used an ad-hoc English-based orthography typical of 18th-century amateur linguists. His system is:
- English-based spelling conventions
- Some use of diacritics (inconsistent)
- No standardized transcription system
- Influenced by his New England English phonology

Cowan (1973) provides a detailed analysis of Stiles' orthographic conventions and their phonetic interpretation.

## 5. Scholarly Treatment

- **Cowan, William (1973)** — "Pequot from Stiles to Speck." *IJAL* 39(3): 164–172. The key secondary source. Cowan compares Stiles' 1762 data with Speck's 1904/1928 data, tracking phonological and lexical changes. Available on JSTOR.

- **Wiktionary appendix** — "A vocabulary of the Pequot Indians" — replicates the manuscript content with numbering following Cowan (1973). Available at: https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:A_vocabulary_of_the_Pequot_Indians

- **NAA MS 5** — Smithsonian National Anthropological Archives holds a copy transcribed for William Wallace Tooker by F.B. Dexter, Assistant Librarian at Yale.

## 8. Key Features

- **Earliest systematic Pequot documentation** (predates Prince-Speck 1904 by 142 years)
- **Numbers 11–1000** — provides crucial data on the numeral system
- **Diachronic value** — Cowan (1973) uses Stiles as baseline for measuring language attrition
- **Y-dialect features** — shows characteristic PA *r → /y/ reflex
- **Post-war context** — documented ~125 years after the Pequot War, from a surviving community

## 9. Research Gaps

- The informant's identity is unknown, limiting sociolinguistic analysis
- No modern critical edition of the manuscript exists (only Cowan's analysis and the Wiktionary transcription)
- The relationship between Stiles' Groton Pequot and the Mohegan variety documented by Fielding needs further study
- Comparison with James Noyes' 1690 Pequot vocabulary (if it survives) would be valuable
