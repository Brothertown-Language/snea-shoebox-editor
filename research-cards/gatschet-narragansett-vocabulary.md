# Research Card: Albert S. Gatschet — Narragansett Vocabulary Collected in 1879

## 1. Primary Source

**Author:** Albert Samuel Gatschet (1832–1907) — Swiss-American ethnologist and linguist. Worked for the U.S. Bureau of American Ethnology under John Wesley Powell. Collected vocabularies of nearly 100 Native American languages.

**Publication:** "Narragansett Vocabulary Collected in 1879" — published posthumously in *International Journal of American Linguistics* 39(1): 14 (January 1973). Edited and introduced by William Cowan.

**Manuscript:** Smithsonian Institution, National Anthropological Archives, MS 23 (Narragansett Vocabulary). Also MS 68 (Gatschet notebook with vocabularies, texts, notes mainly 1878–1879).

## 2. Dialect Affiliation

Narragansett (Eastern Algonquian, Y-dialect) — the language of the Narragansett people of Rhode Island. Closely related to Massachusett/Wampanoag and Mohegan-Pequot.

## 3. Significance

This vocabulary represents the **last known direct elicitation from a speaker** of Narragansett. By 1879, the language had not been natively spoken for decades. Gatschet's informant was likely one of the last individuals with any significant knowledge of the language.

## 4. Estimated Entry Count

The exact count is not specified in the available metadata, but the vocabulary is described as a word list of moderate size. Cowan (1973) used it as the basis for his article "Narragansett 126 Years After" (comparing Gatschet's data with Williams' 1643 data).

## 5. Orthographic System

Gatschet used a phonetic transcription system developed by the Bureau of American Ethnology (the Powell classification system). This is more systematic than the ad-hoc English orthography of the colonial sources, but still reflects late 19th-century phonetic transcription conventions.

## 8. Key Features

- **Last known speaker elicitation** — the closest we have to a "terminal" data point for Narragansett
- **Diachronic value** — Cowan (1973) explicitly compares Gatschet with Williams to measure language change and attrition
- **Systematic transcription** — BAE phonetic system is more reliable than colonial ad-hoc orthography
- **Post-extinction documentation** — documents the state of the language after decades of disuse

## 9. Research Gaps

- The full vocabulary has not been published in a modern critical edition
- Systematic comparison with Williams' 1643 data (beyond Cowan's analysis) has not been done
- The identity and background of Gatschet's informant is not well documented
- The relationship between Gatschet's transcriptions and the colonial sources needs modern Algonquianist analysis
