## Source 1: Edward Winslow — "Good News from New England" (1624)

**Dialect:** Pokanoket (Plymouth area, Massasoit's people)
**Format:** 22 words/phrases scattered throughout narrative text (not a formal vocabulary)
**Source text:** Project Gutenberg #66332; Salvucci ALR Vol. 27 (Young 1841 reprint)
**Orthography:** English-based, pidgin-influenced per Goddard (1977)
**Status:** Earliest published Wampanoag vocabulary (1624)

### Winslow's Linguistic Observations
- Describes the language as "very copious, large, and difficult"
- Notes that he and others "can only just make themselves understood"
- Documents /l/ → /n/ substitution (characteristic of Massachusett phonology)
- Pidginization evident in his recorded forms (Goddard 1977)

## Source 2: William Wood — "New England's Prospect" (1634)

**Dialect:** Massachusett proper (Boston/Neponset area)
**Format:** 262+ entries in a "small Nomenclator" arranged A–W
**Editions:** 1st 1634, 2nd 1635 (with author corrections/marginalia), 3rd 1639
**Source text:** people.umass.edu/aef6000/Texts/Algonquian/Massachusett/Alia/Wood1634
**Letters marked absent:** F, L, R, X ("not used" / "never used")
**Orthography:** Pidgin-influenced per Salvucci; English-based spelling

### Notable entries
- **Body parts:** Boquoquo (head), Matchanne (nose), Mattone (lip), Whonan (tongue), Wawpiske (belly), Keisseanchacke (back of hand)
- **Nature:** Cone (sun), Coepot (ice), Waacoh (day breaks), Wappinne (wind)
- **Animals:** Aunum (dogge), Ausupp (rackoone), Whauksis (foxe), Wawpatucke (goose)
- **Trade:** Kawkenog wampompeage (let me see money), Kettotanese (lend me monie)
- **Food:** Ausomma petuc quanocke (give me some bread)
- **Daily life:** Wigwam (house), An nu ocke (bed), Anno ke nugge (sieve)
- **Numbers:** Up to 20, counting by nights/months; "Eans causuacke" (4 fathoms), "Nisquan" (six)
- **Abstract:** Ascom quom pauputchim (thankes be given to God), Matta (no/not)

### Dialectal markers
- Wood uses distinct orthographic conventions from both Winslow and Eliot
- Salvucci notes this represents a different scribal tradition

## Source 3: Thomas Sheperd (attr.) — "The Day-Breaking" (1647)

**Dialect:** Massachusett proper (Neponset/Nonantum area; sachems Cutchamakin and Waban)
**Format:** 11 scattered words/phrases including two one-sentence prayers
**Authorship:** Anonymous pamphlet; attributed to Sheperd by Pilling (1891), with Wilson and Eliot as co-authors
**Source text:** Sabin reprint 1865; Salvucci ALR Vol. 27 transcription
**Publication info:** London: Richard Cotes, 1647. "The day-breaking, if not the Sun-Rising of the gospell With the Indians in New-England"

### Words & Phrases

| Word/Phrase | English Gloss |
|---|---|
| Evangenesh Jehovah | The Commandements of Jehovah |
| Wigwam | House |
| Musquantum | Very angry |
| Chechainuppan | Tormented alive |
| Wowein wicke Jehovah | Live in all blisse with Jehovah the blessed God |
| Sachem | King |
| Pawwaw | Sorcerors and witches |
| Chepian | The devill |
| Noonatomen | Rejoycing |
| Amanaomen Jehovah tahassen metagh | Take away Lord my Stony heart |
| Cheehesom Jehovah kekowhogkew | Wash Lord my soule [possibly *chechesom] |

### Dialectal notes
- Chepian = Hobbamock/Chepi from Winslow (death spirit)
- Pawwaw = Powah from Winslow (shaman)
- Sachem = consistent across all early sources
- First recorded use of "Wigwam" in print (also appears in Wood 1634)
- The two prayers are the earliest recorded connected Wampanoag sentences

## Source 4: Roger Williams — "A Key into the Language of America" (1643)

**Note:** Narragansett dialect, not Wampanoag/Massachusett — but closely related and extensively cross-referenced
**Size:** ~2,000 entries
**Status:** Referenced for cognates only

---

## Source 5: John Eliot — "Indian Grammar Begun" (1666)

**Dialect:** Massachusett (Natick dialect, standardized orthography)
**Format:** Grammatical description with vocabulary
**Status:** First standardized orthography for the language; basis of Eliot's Bible translation

---

## Source 6: John Cotton Jr. — Indian Vocabulary (1666–1678)

**Dialect:** Martha's Vineyard Wampanoag (separate dialectal variant from mainland Massachusett)
**Manuscript location:** Massachusetts Historical Society (Ms. N-1042)
**Format:** Words and phrases gathered during his mission to Cape Cod and Martha's Vineyard
**Status:** Represents a distinct dialectal tradition

---

## Source 7: Josiah Cotton — Vocabulary of the Massachusett Language (1707–1708)

**Edited by:** John Pickering (1829/1830)
**Format:** 60 leaves: topical vocabularies, dialogues, Creed, Lord's Prayer, sermon, grammatical forms
**Source:** Mass. Hist. Soc. Collections, 3rd ser., Vol. II (1830), pp. 147–257
**Status:** Most substantial post-Eliot lexical manuscript

## Cross-Source Observations

### Time-depth mapping
| Year | Source | Dialect | Entries | Orthographic System |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1624 | Winslow | Pokanoket | ~22 | English ad-hoc |
| 1634 | Wood | Massachusett | ~262 | English ad-hoc |
| 1643 | Williams | Narragansett | ~2,000 | English systematic |
| 1647 | Sheperd | Massachusett | ~11 | English ad-hoc |
| 1666 | Eliot | Massachusett (Natick) | Grammar | Standardized Eliot |
| 1666–78 | Cotton Jr. | Wampanoag (MV) | Lexicon | Ad-hoc + Eliot-influenced |
| 1707–08 | J. Cotton | Massachusett | ~60 leaves | Pickering-edited |

### Phonological phenomena documented across sources
- **l/r/n merger**: Winslow documents "Winsnow" for "Winslow"; Wood marks F, L, R, X as absent; Eliot's orthography confirms the pattern
- **Pidginization**: Goddard (1977) identifies pidgin features in both Winslow and Wood data
- **Pre-Eliot vs. Eliot orthography**: Three distinct scribal conventions (Winslow, Wood, Sheperd) before Eliot's standardization
