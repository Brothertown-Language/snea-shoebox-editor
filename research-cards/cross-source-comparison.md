# Cross-Source Comparison Table

Shared lexical items across Wood (1634) × Winslow (1624) × Sheperd (1647) × Eliot (1666)

---

## 1. SACHEM — Chief

| Source | Form | Context |
|--------|------|---------|
| Winslow (1624) | Sachim, Sachimo | "Sachim" (chief), "Squasachim" (sachim's wife), "Sachimo comaco" (sachim's house) |
| Wood (1634) | Sachem, Sagimore | General term; also "Squaw Sachem" noted |
| Sheperd (1647) | Sachem | "Sachem signifieth a King" |
| Eliot (1666) | Sótchĭm, Sogmŏ | Grammar lists as class II noun; used throughout Bible translation |
| **Analysis:** Early ad-hoc forms (Sachim/Sachem) show vowel variation between /a/ and /i/ in first syllable. Eliot standardizes to Sótchĭm. The /s/ ~ /ʃ/ alternation (Sachem ~ Sogmŏ) reflects Eliot's noting of plural/obviative forms. |

---

## 2. CHEPI / HOBBAMOCK — Devil, death spirit

| Source | Form | Context |
|--------|------|---------|
| Winslow (1624) | Hobbamock, Hobbamoqui | "the devil"; describes as death/healing spirit (later conflated with Christian Devil) |
| Wood (1634) | Hobbamoqui | A term for the devil; appears in the vocabulary |
| Sheperd (1647) | Chepian | "Chepian signifieth the devill" |
| Eliot (1666) | Chepĭan, Chepĭa | Grammar notes as animate noun |
| **Analysis:** Winslow and Wood use the longer compound form Hobba-mock; Sheperd and Eliot use the root Chēpi- alone. This is a pre-Eliot shift in which form was documented. The root meaning is "separated/set apart" — the spirit of the departed, not a Satanic figure. |

---

## 3. WIGWAM — House

| Source | Form | Context |
|--------|------|---------|
| Wood (1634) | Wigwam | Listed in Nomenclator |
| Sheperd (1647) | Wigwam | "Wigwam signifieth an house" |
| Eliot (1666) | Wékŏm, wétch | Wigwam (house) vs weetu (at the house) — locative forms shown |
| **Analysis:** Remarkably stable across all sources. The locative form wétch (< *weki + -ut) shown by Eliot reveals the underlying noun stem *weki-, which doesn't surface in the pre-Eliot ad-hoc forms. The pre-Eliot transcriptions only recorded the uninflected absolutive wigwam. |

---

## 4. MATTA — No / Not

| Source | Form | Context |
|--------|------|---------|
| Winslow (1624) | Matta | "Matta cuts" — not speak; "Matta neen wonckanet namen" — I shall never see |
| Wood (1634) | Matta | Listed as "no/not" |
| Eliot (1666) | Mátta | Grammar lists as negative particle |
| **Analysis:** Completely stable form across 42 years and 3 scribal traditions. No orthographic variation. |

---

## 5. PAWAW / POWAH — Shaman, sorcerer

| Source | Form | Context |
|--------|------|---------|
| Winslow (1624) | Powah | Shaman/healer |
| Sheperd (1647) | Pawwaw | "Pawwaw signifieth Sorcerors and Witches" |
| Eliot (1666) | Pómŏu, Pomŏm | "powaw" or "medicine man" |
| **Analysis:** Vowel alternation /a/ ~ /o/ between pre-Eliot sources (Powah vs Pawwaw). Eliot standardizes to Pómŏu. The underlying root may be related to healing/prophecy (cf. Proto-Algonquian *powa·θa·wa). |

---

## 6. WAMPUM / WAMPOM— Shell beads (money)

| Source | Form | Context |
|--------|------|---------|
| Wood (1634) | Wampompeage | "Wampompeage" — money; "Kawkenog wampompeage" — let me see money |
| Eliot (1666) | Wómpi- | "white" (adj); wampum derived from "wómpi" (white) + "ompsk" (shell) |
| **Analysis:** Wood documents the compound form wampompeage (wampum beads). Eliot shows the root wómpi- "white" which forms the first element. The full compound was opaque to English speakers who shortened it to "wampum." |

---

## 7. KIEHTAN — God (creator spirit)

| Source | Form | Context |
|--------|------|---------|
| Winslow (1624) | Kiehtan | The good creator spirit; distinct from Hobbamock |
| Eliot (1666) | Kéhtian | Used in Bible translation for the Christian God |
| **Analysis:** Winslow documents the pre-Christian usage as the benevolent creator. Eliot appropriates the term for the Christian God. The semantic narrowing is total — from "supreme being" to "the Christian God." |

---

## 8. PIESE / PNIESE — Captain, valiant man

| Source | Form | Context |
|--------|------|---------|
| Winslow (1624) | Pniese, pinese, pinse | Valiant man, captain, councilor |
| Wood (1634) | Pinese | Captain |
| **Analysis:** Survival of the consonant cluster /pn-/ at the beginning of the word (pniese, pinese) is a distinctive Old Wampanoag feature that later was lost or modified in the Eliot-era stage of the language. |

---

## 9. ASUH — Dog

| Source | Form | Context |
|--------|------|---------|
| Wood (1634) | Aunum | Dog |
| Eliot (1666) | Ánŏm | Dog |
| **Analysis:** Wood's "aunum" maps to Eliot's Ánŏm. The diphthong /au/ in Wood represents the same sound that Eliot writes as á — this is an orthographic convention difference, not a phonetic one. Wood heard a offglide that Eliot's system absorbed into the vowel quality mark. |

---

## 10. SQUAW — Woman

| Source | Form | Context |
|--------|------|---------|
| Wood (1634) | Squaw | Woman; "Squaw Sachem" — woman chief |
| Winslow (1624) | Squasachim | "the sachim's wife" (squa + sachim) |
| Eliot (1666) | Skuáw, Sqau | Woman (animate noun) |
| **Analysis:** The consonant cluster /skw-/ is rendered "squ-" consistently across all sources. Winslow attests the compounding use (squa- as woman/female prefix). |

## Key Conclusion

Three pre-Eliot scribal traditions (Winslow, Wood, Sheperd) used different ad-hoc English orthographic strategies. The variations between them reflect both:
- Real dialectal differences (Pokanoket vs Massachusett)
- Different English scribal training and hearing biases
- Lack of a standardized target orthography (which Eliot provided in 1666)

Winslow and Sheperd share more features with each other than either does with Wood. Eliot's 1666 grammar represents a conscious standardization that resolved the pre-existing scribal variation, at the cost of obscuring the earlier dialectal diversity.

The post-Eliot sources (J. Cotton Jr., J. Cotton, E. Mayhew) show increasing adoption of Eliot's orthographic system, with J. Cotton Jr. representing a transitional phase and J. Cotton/E. Mayhew fully adopting the Eliot system. The modern reconstructions (WLRP, Costa, O'Brien) provide phonemic baselines that enable more accurate interpretation of the colonial orthographies.
