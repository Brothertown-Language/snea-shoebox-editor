# Research Card: Experience Mayhew — Indian Converts (1727) and Massachusett Psalter

## 1. Primary Source

**Author:** Experience Mayhew (1673–1758) — New England missionary to the Wampanoag on Martha's Vineyard. Son of John Mayhew, grandson of Thomas Mayhew Jr. (the first English missionary to learn Wampanoag). Fluent in the Wampanoag language. Author of the *Massachusett Psalter* (1709) and *Indian Converts* (1727).

**Indian Converts (1727):** Full title: *Indian Converts: or, Some account of the lives and dying speeches of a considerable number of the Christianized Indians of Martha's Vineyard, in New-England*. Published in London. Contains biographical narratives of four generations of Wampanoag Christians, with Wampanoag words and phrases embedded in the English text.

**Massachusett Psalter (1709):** A translation of the Book of Psalms into the Massachusett language, following Eliot's orthography. One of the few post-Eliot printed works in the language.

## 2. Dialect Affiliation

Martha's Vineyard Wampanoag — the same dialect documented by John Cotton Jr. (1666–1678). Mayhew was a fluent speaker, not just a recorder, which makes his data potentially more reliable than that of non-speaker recorders.

## 3. Estimated Entry Count

- **Indian Converts:** Scattered Wampanoag words and phrases throughout the biographical narratives. Not a formal vocabulary, but contains valuable lexical and phraseological data.
- **Massachusett Psalter:** Full book of Psalms (~150 chapters) translated into Massachusett — a substantial corpus of connected text.

## 4. Orthographic System

Mayhew uses Eliot's standardized orthography (the same system used in the Eliot Bible). As a fluent speaker, his usage is considered reliable. The *Massachusett Psalter* follows Eliot's conventions closely.

## 5. Accessibility Status

- **Indian Converts (1727):** Freely available on Archive.org (multiple copies, including John Carter Brown Library copy)
- **Modern edition:** *Experience Mayhew's Indian Converts: A Cultural Edition* (2008), edited by Laura Arnold Leibman — available via UMass Press and Project MUSE
- **Massachusett Psalter (1709):** Extremely rare; copies at American Antiquarian Society and MHS. Not widely digitized.

## 7. Key Features

- **Fluent speaker data** — Mayhew is unique among colonial recorders in being a fluent Wampanoag speaker
- **Connected text** — the Psalter provides full-sentence corpus data, not just word lists
- **Biographical context** — Indian Converts provides sociolinguistic context for the vocabulary
- **Four generations** — covers Wampanoag Christian community across multiple generations
- **Post-Eliot continuity** — shows the language in use ~40 years after Eliot's Bible

## 8. Research Gaps

- The Massachusett Psalter has not been digitized or transcribed in a modern edition
- Systematic extraction of Wampanoag vocabulary from Indian Converts has not been done
- Comparison of Mayhew's fluent-speaker data with non-speaker recorders (Winslow, Wood) would be valuable
- The relationship between Mayhew's MV Wampanoag and Eliot's Natick Massachusett needs study
