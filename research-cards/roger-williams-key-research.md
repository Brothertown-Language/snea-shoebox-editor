## 1. Roger Williams as Linguist

**Roger Williams (c. 1603–1683)** was a Puritan minister, theologian, and founder of Providence Plantations (later Rhode Island). Banished from Massachusetts Bay Colony in 1635 for his radical views on religious freedom and land rights, he settled among the Narragansett and Wampanoag peoples, learning their languages through extensive daily interaction as a trader, missionary, and diplomat.

Williams' linguistic work is remarkable for its era. Unlike many colonial linguists who imposed Latin grammatical frameworks on Indigenous languages, Williams organized his material **by situational context** (greetings, eating, travel, hunting, etc.), reflecting a usage-based approach centuries ahead of its time. The book served multiple purposes:

- A practical phrasebook for English settlers
- A political document rebutting Massachusetts Bay's claims in *New England's First Fruits* (1643) about Indian conversions to Christianity (see Cesarini 2003)
- An argument for Indigenous land rights — Williams maintained that the King had no right to grant title to Indian land without payment
- An ethnographic record celebrating aspects of Narragansett culture that Williams considered superior to European ways

Williams' friend **Gregory Dexter**, a fellow Rhode Island colonist and former London printer, printed the book in London in 1643. It was the first published colonial study of a Native American language in English and brought Williams significant public attention.

### Key biographical sources:
- LaFantasie, Glenn W., ed. (1988). *The Correspondence of Roger Williams*. 2 vols. Providence: Brown University Press.
- Cesarini, J. Patrick (2003). "The Ambivalent Uses of Roger Williams's 'A Key into the Language of America'". *Early American Literature* 38(3): 469–494.
- Chapin, Howard M., introduction to the 1936 reprint (HathTrust / Providence: John Miller).

## 2. Contents of *A Key into the Language of America*

The book consists of **32 chapters**, each covering a different domain of Narragansett life. Each chapter follows a consistent structure:

1. **Vocabulary and phrases** — Narragansett words and sentences with English translations, arranged as a bilingual dialogue/phrasebook
2. **Ethnographic observations** — brief descriptions of customs, manners, and practices related to the chapter's topic
3. **"Spirituall Observations"** — a closing poem in English verse, reflecting theological and moral lessons Williams drew from his observations

## 3. Phonological Analyses

### 3a. Aubin (1972) — *A Historical Phonology of Narragansett*

**George Francis Aubin** (Brown University, Ph.D. dissertation, 1972, 95 pp.) is the foundational modern phonological analysis of Narragansett. Abstracted in *Dissertation Abstracts International* 33, No. 8A (1973), 4379.

**Key findings (Aubin 1972):**
- Williams' recordings are **substantially accurate** as phonetic transcriptions, though filtered through 17th-century English orthography
- Narragansett consonant system is consistent with Proto-Algonquian (PA) origins
- Voiced stops (*b, d, g*) in Williams' spellings likely represent **lenited allophones** or orthographic variation, not phonemic distinctions
- Confirmed the language as a **Y-dialect** (PA *r → /y/)
- Identified systematic vowel length contrasts preserved in Narragansett

**Aubin's other works on Narragansett:**
- (1972) "Roger Williams: Another View." *IJAL* 38: 266–277.
- (1975) "More on Narragansett *Keesuckquand*." *IJAL* 41: 239–240. https://www.jstor.org/stable/1264545
- (1975) *A Proto-Algonquian Dictionary*. Ottawa: National Museums of Canada.
- (1975) "Narragansett Color Terms." In *Papers of the 7th Algonquian Conference*, ed. W. Cowan, pp. 105–114. Ottawa: Carleton University.
- (1976) "Quelques aspects du système consonantique du narragansett." In *Actes du 8e Congrès des Algonquinistes*, ed. W. Cowan, pp. 151–155. Ottawa: Carleton University.

### 3b. Cowan (1969, 1973)

**William Cowan** published two important analyses:

- (1969) "PA \*a, \*k and \*t in Narragansett." *IJAL* 35: 28–33.
  - Examines specific PA phoneme reflexes in Narragansett
- (1973) "Narragansett 126 Years After." *IJAL* 39(1): 7–13.
  - Revisits the language using the then-recently collected Gatschet vocabulary (see below)

### 3c. Goddard & Bragdon (1988)

**Ives Goddard** and **Kathleen Bragdon**, *Native Writings in Massachusett*. Transactions of the American Philosophical Society vol. 78 (1988).

Part II: **"Phonology and Orthography"** (pp. 474–486) is a primary reference for Massachusett phonology that also provides critical comparative material for Narragansett. Goddard's evaluation of Williams' orthography:

- Williams' spelling system reflects 17th-century English orthographic conventions
- The **accent marks** (discussed in §4 below) are the most detailed suprasegmental notation in any colonial Algonquian source
- Goddard evaluates Williams as **more phonetically accurate than Cotton** (John Eliot's Massachusett Bible translator)

### 3d. Hamp (1970)

**Eric P. Hamp**, "On Nasalization in Narragansett." *IJAL* 36: 58–59.
- Analyzes the intrusive nasal phenomenon (see §5 below)

### 3e. Mierle (1975)

**Shelley Mierle**, "Further Evidence Regarding the Intrusive Nasal in Narragansett." *IJAL* 41: 78–80.
- Builds on Hamp (1970) with additional data on nasalization patterns

### 3f. Gatschet (1879/1973)

**Albert S. Gatschet** collected a Narragansett vocabulary in 1879 from a tribal member, published posthumously:
- Gatschet, Albert S. "Narragansett Vocabulary Collected in 1879." *IJAL* 39(1): 14 (1973).
- This represents the **last known direct elicitation** from a speaker.

### 3g. Brinley (c. 1700s / 1900)

**Francis Brinley**, "Francis Brinley's Briefe Narrative of the Nanhiganset Countrey." *Publications of the Rhode Island Historical Society* 8(2): 69–96 (1900).
- A later colonial source supplementing Williams' data.

## 4. Accent Marks in the Key

Williams used a system of diacritical marks that is **unusually detailed for a colonial Algonquian source** and has been the subject of significant scholarly attention.

### Types of marks:

1. **Acute accent (´)** — appears on vowels, e.g., *Nétop* 'friend', *Wússuck* 'pan'
   - Likely marks **primary stress**
   - Aubin interprets these as indicating stressed syllables

2. **Grave accent (`)** — less frequent, e.g., *Kuttòv-* 
   - Possible secondary stress or tone distinction

3. **Circumflex (^)** — e.g., *Ewô* 'yes'
   - Goddard (in Goddard & Bragdon 1988) interprets this as marking **long vowels** or **falling tone**
   - Aubin suggests it marks **nasalization** in some contexts

4. **Macron (¯)** — indicates vowel length in some words

5. **Tilde (˜)** — used over vowels to indicate **nasalization**, e.g., *ã* in some forms

### Scholarly interpretations:

| Scholar | Interpretation of marks |
|---------|------------------------|
| Aubin (1972) | Stress marking primarily; some length/tone markers |
| Goddard (1988) | Complex system: stress + length + possible pitch |
| O'Brien (2004) | Stress and vowel quality indicators; practical pedagogical transcription |
| Cowan (1969) | Focuses on PA correspondences; treats accents as stress |

### Significance:
Williams' accent system is the **most detailed suprasegmental recording** in any 17th-century Algonquian source, exceeding the Massachusett work of John Eliot or Josiah Cotton in phonetic detail. It provides crucial evidence for the reconstruction of Narragansett prosody. However, the system is not fully understood — different scholars have proposed differing interpretations, and Williams never explained his diacritic conventions explicitly.

## 5. Preaspiration in Narragansett

Preaspiration (phonetic [ʰC], where the release of a preceding vowel or the onset of a consonant involves an [h]-like period) has been proposed as a feature of Narragansett phonology, though it is debated.

### Evidence:

1. **Williams' spellings** sometimes write sequences like *hh* or *h* before stops:
   - E.g., *kehtuck* 'land' (vs. Massachusett *kehteauq*)
   - *ohke* 'earth'

2. **PA correspondences**: PA *hk clusters reflex as [h] + consonant in some Eastern Algonquian languages, and Williams' spellings may reflect preaspirated stops from these clusters

3. **Comparison with related languages:**
   - Massachusett has *hp, ht, hk, hč* series (written as such in Eliot's Bible)
   - Mohegan-Pequot shows preaspiration in cognate forms
   - Preaspiration is phonemic in some modern Algonquian languages (e.g., Plains Cree has preaspirated *hp, ht, hk, hč* as reflexes of PA *hp, hk, hč)

### Scholarly assessment:

- **Aubin (1972)** does not treat preaspiration as a phonemic category in Narragansett, analyzing Williams' *h*-sequences as consonant clusters /hC/
- **Goddard** (in Goddard & Bragdon 1988) recognizes /h/-clusters in Massachusett that correspond to potential preaspiration contexts in Narragansett
- **O'Brien** (2004) transcribes preaspirated stops as sequences (*hp, ht, hk*) in his pedagogical materials

### Status:
The question of preaspiration in Narragansett remains unresolved due to the limitations of the 17th-century orthographic record. The phonetic reality probably involved **allophonic preaspiration** of voiceless stops following vowels, which Williams inconsistently represented.

### General reference on preaspiration as a phonetic phenomenon:
- Wikipedia: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preaspiration

## 6. Proto-Algonquian → Narragansett Correspondences

Narragansett is classified as an **Eastern Algonquian** language, specifically a **Y-dialect** (reflex of PA *r → /y/). Below are the major sound correspondences from Proto-Algonquian (PA) as reconstructed by Aubin (1972, 1975).

### 6.1 Consonants

| PA phoneme | Narragansett reflex | Example (Williams' spelling) | Notes |
|------------|---------------------|------------------------------|-------|
| *p         | p                  | *p* in *paut*~*pit* 'come'   | Retained as voiceless p |
| *t         | t                  | *t* in *tunna* 'mouth'        | Retained as voiceless t; |
|            |                     |                              | Palatalized to tʃ before front vowels? |
| *k         | k                  | *k* in *keén* 'you'           | Retained as k; |
|            |                     |                              | Lenition/loss word-finally |
| *č         | tʃ (written *ch*)  | *ch* in *cháchésu* 'cold'     | Retained |
| *s         | s                  | *s* in *séip* 'river'         | Retained |
| *š         | ʃ (written *sh*)   | *sh* in *shóna* 'money'       | Retained |
| *h         | h                  | *h* in *nétop* 'friend'       | Retained; also in clusters |
| *m         | m                  | *m* in *matta* 'no'           | Retained |
| *n         | n                  | *n* in *nanaw* 'three'        | Retained |
| *w         | w                  | *w* in *wétu* 'house'         | Retained |
| *y         | y (j)              | *y* in *yo* 'here'            | Retained |
| ***r**     | **y**              | **Y-dialect diagnostic**      | **Key isogloss**: PA *r → Narragansett y |
| ***l**     | n                  |                               | Shared with Massachusett; contrast with Mohegan *l |
| ***θ**     | n                  |                               | PA *θ → n in SE New England |
| ***x**     | h                  |                               | PA *x → h in Narragansett |

### 6.2 Y-Dialect isogloss

The PA *r → /y/ reflex defines the **Y-dialect group** within Eastern Algonquian:

| Language | Reflex of PA *r | 
|----------|----------------|
| Narragansett | y (Y-dialect) |
| Mohegan-Pequot | y (Y-dialect) |
| Shinnecock | y (Y-dialect) |
| Massachusett | n (N-dialect) |
| Wampanoag | n (N-dialect) |
| Munsee Delaware | l (L-dialect) |
| Unami Delaware | l (L-dialect) |

### 6.3 PA *k Loss

Cowan (1969) identified conditions under which PA *k is lost in Narragansett:
- PA *k → ∅ / V_V (intervocalic loss) in specific environments
- Contrasts with Massachusett where *k is retained in the same contexts

### 6.4 Supposed "Intrusive Nasal" (Hamp 1970; Mierle 1975)

Williams frequently writes nasal vowels (with tilde or n) where PA reconstruction does not predict a nasal. This has been analyzed as:

- **Hamp (1970):** The "intrusive nasal" is an **archaic retention** of a PA nasal element, not an innovation
- **Mierle (1975):** Additional data supports Hamp's analysis; the nasalization is systematic and follows phonological conditioning

This remains an area of active debate in Algonquian historical linguistics.

### 6.5 Vowels

(Reconstructed from Aubin 1972 and O'Brien 2004)

| PA vowel | Narragansett | Example | Notes |
|----------|--------------|---------|-------|
| *a       | a            | *à*     | Short |
| *ā       | â            | *â*     | Long, sometimes nasalized |
| *e       | ə (written *e*, *u*) | *e*, *u* | Schwa; written variably |
| *i       | i            | *i*     | Short |
| *ī       | î            | *î*     | Long |
| *o       | u, o         | *u*, *o* | Short |
| *ō       | ô            | *ô*     | Long |
| *wa      | o (V_C)      | *o*     | Reduced |

### 6.6 Stress

Williams' accent marks (acute, grave, circumflex; see §4) provide the primary evidence for Narragansett stress. Preliminary patterns:
- **Penultimate stress** appears most common
- **Final syllable stress** on certain morphological categories
- Some evidence for **pitch accent** (circumflex marking)

## 8. Revitalization Efforts

### Status
Narragansett is classified as **extinct** (ISO 639-3: xnt; Glottolog: narr1280). The last known fluent speakers died in the 19th century. The Gatschet vocabulary (1879) is the last direct elicitation from a speaker.

### Primary Revitalization Organization
**Aquidneck Indian Council** — led by Frank Waobu O'Brien (Moondancer) and Julianne Jennings (Strong Woman). Based in Newport, RI.

### Key initiatives:

1. **Language classes** — O'Brien has taught Narragansett through the Aquidneck Indian Council for decades
2. **Pedagogical materials** — *Introduction to the Narragansett Language*, *Indian Grammar Dictionary for N-Dialect*, *Grammatical Studies in the Narragansett Language* — all based on Williams' Key
3. **Facebook page** — "Speaking Our Narragansett Language" provides alphabet, vocabulary, and community engagement
4. **The Narragansett Dawn** (1935–1936) — A tribal publication that included 14 language lessons, edited by tribal members Princess Red Wing and Ernest Hazard. This is the only known published attempt at language teaching by tribal members themselves in the early 20th century.
5. **OLAC resources** — Open Language Archives Community aggregation at http://www.language-archives.org/language/xnt

### Federal Context (2024–2026)
The U.S. Department of the Interior released a **10-Year National Plan on Native Language Revitalization** (Dec 2024) with ~$1.5B/year proposed investment (2025–2035), including 100 language nests, 100 K-12 immersion schools, and direct tribal funding. https://www.bia.gov/sites/default/files/media_document/final_12032024_10-year_national_plan_on_native_language_revitalization.508.pdf

### Criticisms and challenges
- The extant corpus (~2,000 words, no fluent speakers) is insufficient for **full grammatical reconstruction** without heavy reliance on comparative evidence from related languages (Massachusett, Mohegan-Pequot)
- **Pronunciation** cannot be fully recovered from written records — prosody and many segmental details remain speculative
- Colonial source limitations: Williams' orthography is filtered through 17th-century English phonology
- Funding shortages — the Narragansett Indian Tribal Historic Preservation Office (NITHPO) reported in 2025 that BIA funding was substantially absent
