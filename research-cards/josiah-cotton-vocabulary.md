# Research Card: Josiah Cotton — Vocabulary of the Massachusett Language (1707–1708)

## 1. Primary Source

**Author:** Josiah Cotton (1680–1756) — Massachusetts judge, magistrate, and grandson of John Cotton the Puritan minister. Compiled the vocabulary in 1707–1708 as a personal linguistic project. Not a missionary, but a civic official with interest in Native languages.

**Manuscript:** 60 leaves containing: topical vocabularies, dialogues, the Creed, Lord's Prayer, a sermon, and grammatical forms. Compiled at Plymouth, Massachusetts.

**Published edition:** Edited by John Pickering (1777–1846), published as "Vocabulary of the Massachusetts (or Natick) Indian Language" in *Collections of the Massachusetts Historical Society*, 3rd ser., Vol. II (1830), pp. 147–257. Also issued as a separate offprint in 1829.

**Full text access:**
- Archive.org (1829 offprint): https://archive.org/details/vocabularyofmass00cott
- HathiTrust: https://catalog.hathitrust.org/Record/100280131
- Library of Congress: https://www.loc.gov/resource/gdcmassbookdig.vocabularyofmass00cott/
- Wikimedia Commons (PDF): https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Vocabulary_of_the_Massachusetts_(or_Natick)_Indian_language_(IA_vocabularyofmass00cott).pdf

## 2. Dialect Affiliation

Massachusett (Natick dialect) — the same variety documented by John Eliot in his Bible translation and grammar. Cotton's vocabulary represents the **post-Eliot** stage of the language, approximately 40 years after Eliot's *Indian Grammar Begun* (1666). This makes it valuable for studying diachronic change within the Massachusett language.

## 3. Structure and Contents

The vocabulary is organized topically, following the tradition of Roger Williams' *Key*:

- **Topical vocabularies** — words grouped by semantic domain (body parts, animals, plants, etc.)
- **Dialogues** — conversational phrases and sentences
- **Creed** — the Christian creed in Massachusett
- **Lord's Prayer** — in Massachusett
- **Sermon** — a short sermon in the language
- **Grammatical forms** — noun declensions, verb conjugations

This structure makes it one of the most comprehensive post-Eliot lexical manuscripts.

## 4. Estimated Entry Count

Approximately 60 leaves (120 pages) of vocabulary and grammatical material. The Pickering edition runs to 110 pages in the MHS Collections format. This is the most substantial post-Eliot lexical manuscript for Massachusett.

## 5. Orthographic System

Cotton uses Eliot's standardized orthography (the same system used in the Eliot Bible and *Indian Grammar Begun*), with some personal variations:

- Uses the Eliot ⟨ꝏ⟩ (oo ligature) for /uː/
- Uses accent marks for vowel length (following Eliot's system)
- Preaspirated stops written as ⟨hp, ht, hk, hč⟩ (following Eliot's convention)
- Some idiosyncratic spellings reflecting Cotton's own hearing or dialectal differences

The Pickering edition normalizes some of Cotton's orthography to early 19th-century typographic conventions, which may obscure some original features.

## 8. Key Features

- **Most substantial post-Eliot lexical manuscript** for Massachusett
- Includes **connected text** (dialogues, prayers, sermon) — not just word lists
- **Grammatical forms** provide morphological data beyond simple vocabulary
- Pickering's edition includes **English-Massachusett index** (valuable for reverse lookup)
- Represents the language **~40 years after Eliot's grammar**, potentially showing language change

## 9. Research Gaps

- No modern linguistic edition exists (Pickering's 1829 edition is the only published version)
- The relationship between Cotton's orthography and Eliot's needs systematic comparison
- Dialectal differences between Cotton's Plymouth-area Massachusett and Eliot's Natick dialect have not been systematically analyzed
- The grammatical forms section has not been analyzed with modern Algonquianist methodology
