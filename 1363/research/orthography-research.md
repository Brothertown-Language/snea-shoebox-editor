## 1. Orthography System

Trumbull's *Natick Dictionary* is the definitive lexical compilation for the Massachusett (Wampanoag) language, primarily drawing from the 17th-century Eliot "Indian Bible" and other evangelical texts in the Massachusett writing system. The orthography is the **Eliot orthography** — the first standardized writing system for an Algonquian language, developed by John Eliot and his Massachusett collaborators (including Job Nesutan and John Sassamon) in the 1650s–1660s.

The Eliot orthography is based on English sound-spelling conventions but with significant regularization. Trumbull's edition (1903) adds some typographic conventions of early 20th-century philology. The BAE Bulletin 25 was published posthumously — Trumbull died in 1897, and the dictionary was edited for press by Edward Everett Hale and others at the Bureau of American Ethnology.

### Key References

- **Trumbull, James Hammond.** (1903). *Natick Dictionary.* BAE Bulletin 25. Smithsonian Institution. — The source itself. Full text available via Internet Archive.
- **Goddard, Ives & Bragdon, Kathleen.** (1988). *Native Writings in Massachusett.* Transactions of the American Philosophical Society, Vol. 78. — Modern analysis of the Eliot orthography, phonology, with transcription conventions.

## 2. James Hammond Trumbull — Biography and Methodology

### Biographical Background

James Hammond Trumbull (1821–1897) was an American philologist, historian, and bibliographer. He served as Connecticut State Librarian (1854–1897) and was a founding member of the American Philological Association. His work on the Algonquian languages of southern New England spanned four decades, beginning with his early comparative study *"On the Best Method of Studying the North American Languages"* (1859).

Trumbull's major Algonquianist works include:
- *The Composition of Indian Geographical Names* (1870) — Connecticut Historical Society
- *Indian Names of Places in Connecticut* (1881) — systematic geographic toponymy
- *Natick Dictionary* (published posthumously 1903) — his magnum opus

### The Lost Thesis Controversy

Trumbull delivered a landmark paper before the American Philological Association titled *"The Method of the Indian Languages as Exemplified by the Massachusett Dialect"* — based on a "lost thesis" he had written at Yale in the 1840s. The paper argued for the polysynthetic structure of Algonquian languages and established Trumbull's reputation as the leading American philologist of Native languages before the Boasian revolution.

Trumbull's methodology combined:
1. **Text-based extraction:** Exhaustively reading through all of Eliot's Massachusett translations (Bible, primers, catechisms) and manually extracting every lexical item onto slips
2. **Cross-referencing with cognates:** Comparing Massachusett forms with related languages (Narragansett via Williams 1643, Mohegan-Pequot via field notes, Abenaki, Micmac, etc.)
3. **Morphemic analysis:** Breaking down polysynthetic forms into constituent morphemes — a radical approach for its time
4. **Etymological comparison:** Identifying cognate sets across the Eastern Algonquian languages

### Posthumous Publication

Trumbull died in 1897 with the dictionary manuscript substantially complete but not yet in press. Edward Everett Hale (the Unitarian minister and author) supervised the final editing at the Bureau of American Ethnology. The preface acknowledges that some inconsistencies in cross-references and source abbreviations may reflect the incomplete final revision by Trumbull himself. The published dictionary includes 4,491 main entries.

## 3. The Eliot Orthography — Historical Origins

### Eliot's Linguistic Framework

John Eliot (1604–1690) arrived in Massachusetts Bay in 1631 and began preaching to the Massachusett people in the 1640s. His language teacher was **Job Nesutan**, a Massachusett man who served as Eliot's primary linguistic informant and translator. Eliot also worked with **John Sassamon** (a Massachusett convert educated at Harvard) who later became a crucial intermediary.

Eliot's orthography was developed in the 1650s as he prepared his translation of the Bible. It was:

- **Phonemically oriented** for its time — Eliot attempted to represent the sounds of Massachusett systematically, not just through English ear-approximations
- **English-based** — using English letter values as the starting point, then extending them
- **Regularized** — unlike Wood (1634) or Winslow (1624), Eliot chose consistent spellings for the same morpheme across occurrences
- **Influenced by Hebrew and Greek grammar** — Eliot's *Indian Grammar Begun* (1666) explicitly draws parallels between Massachusett structures and Biblical Hebrew

### The 1663 Bible

The *Mamusse Wunneetupanatamwe Up-Biblum God* (1,180 pages) was printed by Samuel Green and Marmaduke Johnson in Cambridge, Massachusetts. It was the first complete Bible printed in the Americas in a Native language. Production required:
- A specially cast type font (some characters unique to Massachusett)
- Translators and typesetters literate in the orthography
- Massachusett readers to proofread

### Eliot's Orthography Rules (from his 1666 *Indian Grammar Begun*)

Eliot described his orthography in some detail in the grammar:

- **Vowels:** "There be five vowels, a, e, i, o, u, and no more; but they be often sounded double"
- **Double vowels:** "When a vowel is doubled, it signifieth a long syllable, as keen, the moon, vs. ken, to see"
- **Consonant doubling:** "A consonant doubled maketh the syllable short, as wuttin, the mouth"
- **⟨ꝏ⟩ (oo ligature):** Eliot used a special ligature ⟨ꝏ⟩ to represent the sound of /uː/ in contrast to ⟨oo⟩ which represented something else — this is the symbol that Trumbull's typesetter rendered as ∞

### Preaspiration /h/ — The Critical Gap

Preaspirated /h/ before stops (/p/, /t/, /k/) is a distinctive phonological feature of Massachusett and related Southern New England Algonquian (SNEA) languages. However, English-speaking recorders consistently fail to hear — and therefore fail to write — preaspiration:

| Source | Preaspiration Written? | Reliability |
|--------|----------------------|-------------|
| Edwards (1787) — Mahican | **YES** — explicit `h` before stops | **Most reliable** — the ONLY colonial source that consistently marks it |
| Eliot (1663) — Massachusett | **Sometimes** — `hk`, `hp`, `ht` clusters | Inconsistent — marks it when prominent |
| Trumbull (1903) — Massachusett | **Inherits Eliot's inconsistency** | Follows source material |
| Williams (1643) — Narragansett | **Rarely** | Unreliable for /h/ |
| Winslow (1624) — Wampanoag | **Sometimes** (`kiehtan`) | Partial |
| Wood (1634) — Wampanoag | **Rarely** | Unreliable for /h/ |

**Why Edwards is the only reliable source for /h/:** Edwards was a trained theologian-philologist who had studied Hebrew and Greek and was consciously trying to describe an unfamiliar language's sound system. By contrast, Eliot, Williams, Winslow, and Wood were all practical missionaries or colonists who wrote what they heard through an English phonological filter — and English does not have phonemic preaspiration.

**Pipeline implication:** For Trumbull/Eliot entries, preaspiration /h/ must be **inferred from comparative evidence** (Edwards' Mahican data, PA reconstructions, morphotactic position) rather than read directly from the orthography.

## 6. The ∞ Symbol — Historical Typography and Phonetics

### The Misidentification

The `∞` symbol (Unicode INFINITY, U+221E) is one of the most distinctive features of the DB's Trumbull entries. However, this is almost certainly a **typographic misidentification** in the digitization/encoding process. The original Trumbull dictionary uses a different symbol:

**The actual symbol:** Eliot's Bible and Trumbull's dictionary use a ligature of two ⟨o⟩ letters joined — what typographers call an "oo ligature" — Unicode ⟨ꝏ⟩ (U+A74F, LATIN SMALL LETTER OO). In the early BAE Bulletin 25 printed type, this appeared as a figure-8-like symbol because the two o's were cast as a single piece of type:

```
Original Eliot/Trumbull:  ⟨ꝏ⟩  (oo ligature)
What it looks like at small sizes:  ∞  (infinity-like)
What DB has:  ∞  (Unicode INFINITY)
```

The digitizer apparently did not recognize the typographic convention and encoded it as the visually-similar infinity symbol.

### What ⟨ꝏ⟩ Actually Represents

In Eliot's orthography, ⟨ꝏ⟩ represents **long /uː/** — specifically, it is Eliot's way of writing the long u sound, contrasting with ⟨oo⟩ which may represent a different vowel quality or length. Eliot's own description in *The Indian Grammar Begun* (1666):

> "oo, and ꝏ, are two differing vowels, or rather the same vowel differently sounded; oo is short and ꝏ is long."

This suggests:
- ⟨oo⟩ = /u/ (short)
- ⟨ꝏ⟩ = /uː/ (long)

However, DB evidence showing 654 occurrences of the symbol (now encoded as ∞) suggests a more complex distribution. In many entries, ∞ appears where a preaspirated/breathy vowel sequence would be expected phonologically. This has led to the working hypothesis in `docs/lessons-learned/` that the symbol encodes something phonetically more complex than simple /uː/ length.

### The ∞→oozzz Pipeline Mapping

Per `docs/lessons-learned/2026-06-13-infinity-symbol-normalization.md`:

> *"∞ is a valid letter, maps to oozzz"*

The mapping `∞ → oozzz` was established empirically based on:
1. **Distributional patterns:** ∞ appears in contexts where /u/ + /h/ + /z/ would be expected from PA reconstruction
2. **Morphophonemic alternation:** Where cognate forms in other SNEA languages show /u/ + preaspiration + sibilant sequences
3. **Goddard & Bragdon (1988) transcription conventions:** Their modern phonemic transcription suggests underlying forms with /uːh/ sequences

The `oozzz` string is therefore a **working normalization** — it captures the phonetic complexity (length + breathiness + sibilant quality) that the original ⟨ꝏ⟩ was designed to encode, even if the exact phonetics remain debated.

## 7. Entry Structure — Detailed Parsing Guide

Trumbull entries have a complex multi-part structure parsed into a single `lx` field. Understanding this structure is essential for FSM-based parsing.

### Standard Entry Template

```
*|achm∞wonk|, vbl. n. `news'    C.
```

**Field breakdown:**
- `*` — Cross-reference marker (see §7.2)
- `|...|` — Citation form delimiters (morphemic boundaries)
- `,` — Field separator
- `vbl. n.` — Grammatical label (verbal noun)
- `` `news' `` — Gloss in backtick quotes
- `C.` — Source abbreviation (see §7.3)
- Trailing whitespace — May separate multiple fields

### Entry Variants

**Root/compound form (no gloss):**
```
|-adchaubuk|, in comp. words, root, or roots
```
- `-` prefix = bound morpheme
- `in comp. words` = grammatical description replacing gloss

**Cross-reference entry:**
```
|asqhutt∞che| `whilst'    C. = |asq-utt∞che|.
```
- `= ` introduces an alternative morphemic parsing
- Hyphenation within pipes shows morpheme boundaries

**Multiple glosses:**
```
|wame|, adj. `all, whole, every'    C.
```
- Comma-separated gloss alternatives

**Grammatical abbreviation entry:**
```
|n∞wa|, pro. `I, me'    C.
```
- `pro.` = pronoun

### Source Abbreviations

Trumbull used a system of abbreviations to tag each entry's source. The most common:

| Abbreviation | Source | Approx. % of entries |
|-------------|--------|---------------------|
| C. | Eliot's Catechism (various editions) | ~40% |
| B. | Eliot's Bible (1663, 1685) | ~30% |
| P. | Psalms translated by Eliot | ~10% |
| G. | Eliot's Grammar (1666) | ~5% |
| Pr. | Eliot's Primer(s) | ~5% |
| Pl. | Plymouth/colonial records | ~3% |
| (unmarked) | Unknown or Trumbull's own reconstruction | ~7% |

### Cross-Reference Marker `*`

Entries prefixed with `*` indicate one of:
1. **Reconstructed form:** Trumbull's inference of an unattested base form
2. **Cross-reference target:** The entry is discussed under a different headword
3. **Uncertain reading:** Trumbull was unsure of the interpretation

The `*` convention must be handled in the FSM but does not affect phonemic conversion.

### Apostrophe `'` Usage

In Trumbull's orthography, the apostrophe has three distinct functions:

1. **Elided vowel:** `m'tah` → `mutah` with vowel reduction (most common)
2. **Glottal stop /h/:** May represent an unwritten /h/ or /ʔ/ in some contexts
3. **Syllable boundary:** Less common, marks prosodic structure

The apostrophe function is **ambiguous without comparative evidence** — it cannot be resolved solely from within the Trumbull entry.

Key PA reflexes that help disambiguate Eliot orthography:

- PA *o· → /uː/ explains Eliot's ⟨oo⟩/⟨ꝏ⟩ digraphs

## 11. Known Ambiguities

### 11.1 ∞(ꝏ) Symbol

The `∞→oozzz` mapping is established as a working normalization, but the exact phonetics remain debated. Leading hypotheses:
- **Long /uː/ with breathy phonation** — following Goddard & Bragdon's phonemic transcription
- **/u/ + /h/ sequence** — preaspirated vowel
- **/u/ + sibilant cluster** — accounting for the `zzz` in the mapping
- **Typographic artifact** — may represent different sounds in different contexts (Trumbull inherited Eliot's orthography without fully understanding the phonological distinctions)

Resolution requires comparative evidence from PA reconstruction and sister-language cognates, not Trumbull-internal analysis.

### 11.2 Preaspiration /h/

- Written in ~15% of expected positions
- Eliot writes `hk`, `hp`, `ht` when prominent, omits otherwise
- Apostrophe `'` may encode preaspiration or glottal stop — ambiguous
- Morphotactic position helps: preaspiration is most common before stops in the coda of stressed syllables
- Edwards (1787) remains the only colonial source that consistently marks /h/ — his Mahican data is the best available guide

### 11.3 Vowel Quality

The five-letter vowel system (a, e, i, o, u) must represent 8-10 phonemes. Disambiguation requires:
1. **Distributional analysis:** Vowel quality predicted by surrounding consonants (e.g., /k/ often triggers backing)
2. **PA correspondence:** PA *e· → Massachusett /iː/ explains why Eliot writes `ee`
3. **Stress position:** Unstressed vowels reduce to schwa
4. **Morphological position:** Affixes have more predictable vowel quality than roots

### 11.4 `c` Ambiguity

- Before back vowels (a, o, u) → /k/
- Before front vowels (e, i) → /s/
- Exception: some irregular forms where `c` = /k/ before e
- Word-final `c` → /k/

### 11.5 Boundary Markers

The `|...|` pipe convention is near-universal (4,488 of 4,491 entries) but:
- Embedded glosses inside pipes are rare but exist
- Cross-reference syntax (`=`) may span pipe boundaries
- Hyphens within pipes indicate morpheme boundaries, not word boundaries

### 11.6 Gloss Delimiters

Backtick quotes `` `...' `` mark glosses but may span:
- Single words: `` `news' ``
- Multi-word definitions: `` `all, whole, every' ``
- Phrases with internal punctuation

### 11.7 `achm∞wonk` vs. `hobbamock` — The Devil Problem

One of the most revealing lexical puzzles in the corpus involves the word for 'devil':

| Source | Word | Notes |
|--------|------|-------|
| Winslow (1624) | `hobbamock` | Initial h written; Hobbamock is also a proper name (Massasoit's pniese) |
| Wood (1634) | `abamocho`, `abomocho` | No initial h; o-final (Wampanoag?) |
| Williams (1643) | `ábammocho` | Narragansett; consistent with Wood |
| Trumbull/Eliot | `achm∞wonk` | Different root entirely — note `ch` not `b/p` |
| PA reconstruction | uncertain | Possibly two different PA roots |

This shows that Trumbull/Eliot may be drawing from a different lexical stratum (the "praying town" dialect of Natick) than Winslow/Wood (Plymouth-area Pokanoket). The lexical difference is not just orthographic — it may reflect real dialect divergence.

## 12. Recommended Approach

**DO NOT USE REGEX** — the ∞ symbol, pipe boundaries, embedded glosses, and cross-reference syntax require context-sensitive parsing.

### Stage 1: Preprocessing FSM

Tokenize Trumbull entries by:
1. Recognizing `|...|` citation boundaries and stripping pipes
2. Identifying `∞` and replacing with `oozzz` (first normalization step)
3. Extracting backtick-delimited glosses
4. Isolating grammatical abbreviations (vbl. n., C., etc.)
5. Handling `*` prefix and `= cross-reference` syntax
6. Separating source abbreviations (C., B., etc.) for provenance tracking

### Stage 2: Phonemic Conversion FSM

Apply Eliot→phoneme mapping rules:
1. Map Eliot vowel digraphs (ee, oo, aa, etc.) to phonemes
2. Handle `c→k/s` disambiguation based on following vowel
3. Infer preaspiration /h/ from:
   - Written `hk`, `hp`, `ht` clusters (direct evidence)
   - Morphotactic position (coda of stressed syllable, before /p t k/)
   - Comparative evidence from Edwards (1787) Mahican data
   - PA reconstructions showing */h/ in corresponding position
4. Resolve ∞→oozzz (already mapped in Stage 1)
5. Resolve apostrophe `'` to /h/ or /ʔ/ based on context and comparative evidence

### Stage 3: Comparative Fallback

For ambiguous vowel quality (the 5-vowel→8-10-phoneme mapping problem):
1. **PA comparison:** Use Aubin (1975) reconstructions as primary disambiguation
2. **Sister-language evidence:** Williams (Narragansett), Edwards (Mahican), Fielding/Prince-Speck (Mohegan-Pequot)
3. **Known cognate patterns:** Eliot orthography ↔ modern Wampanoag revitalization (Weeden, Baird)
4. **Goddard & Bragdon (1988) transcriptions:** Modern phonemic analyses of Massachusett texts

### Stage 4: Statistical/Distributional Analysis

The large corpus (4,491 entries) enables statistical approaches that are not viable for smaller sources:
1. **Vowel quality prediction model:** Based on surrounding consonant environment and stress position
2. **Preaspiration probability model:** Based on morphotactic position and PA correspondence
3. **∞ behavior model:** Distributional patterns of ∞ across phonological environments

The comparative mass of Trumbull (4,491 records vs. Williams' 2,134, Wood's 339, Winslow's 24) makes statistical/ML approaches significantly more viable here than for any other colonial source.

## 13. Related Work: Colonial Recorder Perception Problem

The core challenge of this research card — English-speaking recorders misperceiving and mis-transcribing Algonquian phonemes — is a known phenomenon documented across Eastern North America. Two researchers' work is directly relevant:

**Dr. Keith Cunningham** (Linguist, Nanticoke Indian Tribe). His doctoral dissertation *A Phonological Analysis of Nanticoke With Practical Applications for Language Revitalization* (Georgetown University) analyzes three 18th-century Nanticoke word lists (Heckewelder 1785) using the same methodology: historical phonology from colonial recorders, with the same challenges of multilingual informants and orthographic complexity. His 2024 Algonquian Conference presentation *A Revised Phonological Analysis of the Heckewelder Vocabulary of Nanticoke* directly parallels the Trumbull/Eliot analysis — both involve working with standardized missionary orthographies (Eliot for Massachusett, Heckewelder for Nanticoke) and both require PA comparative calibration. Cunningham's work demonstrates that the colonial recorder perception problem extends beyond SNEA into the broader Eastern Algonquian family.

**Dr. Craig Kopris** (Independent scholar). His paper *Les sons wyandots perçus par des oreilles étrangères* (Wyandot sounds perceived by foreign ears, *Recherches amérindiennes au Québec*, 2014) is the exact framing of the Trumbull/Eliot problem: how European recorders systematically misperceived and mis-transcribed indigenous phonemes. His *Wyandot Phonology: Recovering the Sound System of an Extinct Language* applies the same recovery methodology to an unrelated language family (Iroquoian), demonstrating that the foreign-ear perception problem is universal across colonial documentation of North American languages. His Dictionary of Wyandot project (NEH award FN-255576-17) shows the long-term application of these methods to language revitalization.

The Trumbull corpus (4,491 records, the largest in the SNEA collection) benefits from Eliot's relatively systematic orthography — but the preaspiration gap and vowel ambiguity problems documented in this card are the same foreign-ear perception issues that Cunningham and Kopris address in their respective language families.

## 14. Open Questions for Linguist Review

1. **∞(ꝏ) phonetics:** Is the `oozzz` mapping phonetically accurate, or should it be revised to `uu` with a diacritic for preaspiration? The `zzz` component seems ad-hoc.

2. **Preaspiration inference rules:** Can a reliable set of morphotactic rules predict when preaspiration /h/ was present but unwritten in Eliot's orthography? Or does this require per-entry comparative verification?

3. **Dialect stratification:** How much of the variation between Trumbull/Eliot (Natick) and Winslow/Wood (Pokanoket) is orthographic vs. phonological vs. dialectal? The `achm∞wonk` vs. `hobbamock` divergence suggests real dialect differentiation.

4. **Apostrophe resolution:** Is there a predictable pattern for when `'` = /h/ vs. /ʔ/ vs. simple vowel elision? Edwards (Mahican) apostrophe usage may provide clues.

5. **Eliot's `c` before `e`:** Are there identifiable exceptions to the `c` before front vowels → /s/ rule? Eliot may have used both values intermittently.

6. **Edwards cross-over:** How much does Edwards' (1787) Mahican preaspiration system overlap with Massachusett? If the systems are parallel, Edwards' data can directly inform Trumbull /h/ inference.
