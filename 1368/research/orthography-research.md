## 1. Publication History and Citation Clarification

**The glossary was published twice in slightly different venues, which causes persistent citation confusion:**

| Publication | Year | Citation | Notes |
|-------------|------|----------|-------|
| **First printing** | 1904 | *American Anthropologist* n.s. Vol 6, No. 1, pp. 18–45 | Jan–Mar 1904 issue. Contains the complete glossary (446 words). |
| **Reprint** | 1906 | *Boas Anniversary Volume* (G. E. Stechert, New York), pp. 18–45 | The same text was reprinted in the Boas Festschrift. |

The Boas volume was not published until 1906 even though its title page reads 1906. Because the glossary appeared first in *American Anthropologist* in 1904, that is the correct year for scholarly citation. The 1906 reprint is typographically identical — same page numbering, same text — which is why later references often cite the Boas volume without noticing the earlier AA publication.

Prince himself published a separate, shorter article: "A Singular Mohegan-Pequot Form" in *American Anthropologist* n.s. Vol 9 (1907), pp. 496–497 (cited in the natick.txt seed data).

**Exact title:** "Glossary of the Mohegan-Pequot Language" (not "The Mohegan-Pequot Language" as sometimes mis-cited). The work is a glossary with brief ethnographic introduction, not a grammar.

## 2. J. Dyneley Prince (1868–1945): The Transcriber

### Background

John Dyneley Prince was a remarkable polymath: diplomat, politician, and academic linguist.

| Role | Details |
|------|---------|
| Born | 1868, New York City |
| Education | Columbia University (A.B. 1888, Ph.D. 1892); also studied in Europe |
| Academic career | Professor of Germanic Languages at Columbia (later Dean) |
| Political career | New Jersey State Senator, U.S. Minister to Denmark (1921–1926), U.S. Minister to Yugoslavia (1926–1932) |
| Languages known | Reported to know ~30 languages including Estonian, Turkish, Arabic, Slavic languages, Hungarian |
| Died | 1945 |

### Linguistic Training and Its Impact on Transcription

Prince was trained in the **Neogrammarian (Junggrammatiker)** tradition, which dominated Germanic linguistics in the late 19th century. This background has specific consequences for his transcription:

**1. Phonetic impressionism over phonemic analysis:**
Neogrammarians believed in recording "exact" phonetic detail rather than phonemic contrasts. Prince's transcription therefore:
- Varies between `a`, `ah`, `au`, `o` for what is likely a single phoneme /aː/ or /ɔː/
- Writes vowel length inconsistently — sometimes with `h`, sometimes with doubling, sometimes not at all
- Records allophonic variation as if it were phonemic

**2. Germanic spelling conventions imposed on English-based transcription:**
Prince had spent his career teaching Germanic philology. He unconsciously applied German orthographic habits even when intending to use English spelling:
- See §5 for full German influence analysis

**3. No IPA training:**
The glossary predates the International Phonetic Alphabet (1888/1900). Prince had no standardized phonetic alphabet — he used a blended English-German ad-hoc system.

**Critical distinction:** Prince was NOT a fieldworker. He transcribed from Speck's field notes. He never heard the language spoken himself except possibly in brief demonstration. This means every transcription is two steps removed from the speaker: Fidelia Fielding → Speck's ears → Speck's field notation → Prince's desk transcription → published form.

## 3. Frank G. Speck (1881–1950): The Fieldworker

### Background

| Role | Details |
|------|---------|
| Born | 1881, New York |
| Education | Columbia University, student of Franz Boas (Ph.D. 1905) |
| Career | Prolific anthropologist of Eastern Algonquian peoples; taught at University of Pennsylvania |
| Specialization | Material culture, ethnography of Algonquian peoples (Penobscot, Mohegan, Nanticoke, Tutelo, etc.) |
| Died | 1950 |

Speck was a Boasian anthropologist whose fieldwork methodology was ethnographic rather than strictly linguistic. His linguistic training was minimal compared to Prince, but he had direct exposure to speakers that Prince never had.

### Relationship with Fidelia Fielding

Speck conducted fieldwork with **Fidelia Fielding (Aiji, 1827–1908)**, the last fluent speaker of Mohegan-Pequot. Fielding was:

- Born in 1827 on the Mohegan reservation in Uncasville, Connecticut
- A direct descendant of the Mohegan chief Uncas
- The keeper of Mohegan traditions — she maintained a diary in Mohegan using her own spelling system
- A monolingual Mohegan speaker as a child; learned English later in life
- Known for her fierce preservation of the language — she refused to speak English with other Mohegans

Fielding was literate in English and developed her own orthography for recording Mohegan, primarily in her personal diaries. Her spelling system was based on English conventions as she understood them, with considerable idiosyncrasy.

### Speck's Field Methods

Speck collected word lists and phrases from Fielding through direct elicitation. His field notes likely recorded:
- Phonetic approximations (Speck had some phonetic training but not at Prince's level)
- English glosses that may not capture precise semantic boundaries
- Possibly some grammatical notes (though these are minimal in the published glossary)

The fieldwork likely occurred between 1900–1903 when Speck was a student at Columbia and making trips to the Mohegan reservation. Speck was in his early 20s at the time.

### The Speck–Prince Handoff

The exact chain of custody for the data is:
1. Fidelia Fielding produces utterances in Mohegan
2. Frank Speck hears and records them in field notation
3. Speck provides his field notes to J. Dyneley Prince
4. Prince converts Speck's notation into the published orthography
5. Prince writes the introductory notes and publishes under both names

This two-stage recording (fieldworker → transcriber) introduces compounding transcription errors:
- Speck's hearing of Fielding's speech (accent, speed, dialect)
- Speck's field notation choices (ad-hoc English-based spelling)
- Prince's interpretation of Speck's notation (he never heard the original)
- Prince's application of German orthographic conventions
- Typesetting errors in 1904 publication (no author proofreading after typesetting)

## 4. Fidelia Fielding's Orthography and Its Influence

Fidelia Fielding kept extensive diaries in Mohegan using **English-based orthography** that she developed herself. Prince and Speck explicitly state that they sometimes deferred to Fielding's spelling for "sentimental reasons" — i.e., they kept her English-based spelling even when a more phonetic transcription would have been technically better.

### Fielding's Diary Spelling Conventions

From analyses of her diaries, Fielding's orthography shows:

| Feature | Example | Notes |
|---------|---------|-------|
| English vowel letters for approximate sounds | `a`, `e`, `i`, `o`, `u` as in English orthography | No consistent length marking |
| `gh` for /k/ or /x/ | Occasional use following English convention | May reflect actual velar fricative |
| `w` for /w/ and as glide | Consistent with English | |
| Doubled consonants | Occasional geminate marking | May reflect actual length or stress |
| No systematic nasalization marking | | Nasal vowels were likely present but unwritten |

### When "Sentimental Reasons" Overrode Accuracy

The Prince-Speck preface indicates they intentionally preserved some of Fielding's spellings because she was the last speaker and her written form had cultural value. This means:

- Some words in the glossary may be Fielding's **written** spelling rather than Prince's **hearing-based** transcription
- These words may reflect English-based sound-to-spelling mappings that differ from what a trained linguist would produce
- The distinction between "written by Fielding" and "recorded by Speck/Prince" is not marked in the glossary

### Impact on Phonemic Reconstruction

Fielding's orthography complicates phonemic reconstruction because:
- Her spelling may be conservative (reflecting older pronunciation she learned as a child)
- Or it may be anglicized (reflecting her English literacy)
- We cannot distinguish between Fielding's "spelling pronunciations" and Prince's phonetic notation

## 5. Detailed German Orthographic Influence

Prince's Germanistic training is visible throughout the transcription. This section catalogs specific German conventions that appear in the Prince-Speck glossary.

### 5.1 Affricate Representations

| Prince-Speck | German convention | English equivalent | Phoneme |
|-------------|-------------------|-------------------|---------|
| `tsh` | German *tsch* (as in *deutsch*) | Rare, used in "batsha" | /tʃ/ |
| `z` | German *z* = /ts/ | English *z* = /z/ | ambiguous |

The use of `tsh` for the /tʃ/ affricate is a distinctly Germanic choice. English colonial recorders typically used `ch` or `tch`. The `ts` prefix represents a German-trained linguist's desire to show the affricate's stop onset.

### 5.2 Sibilant Representations

| Prince-Speck | German convention | Notes |
|-------------|-------------------|-------|
| `sch` (if present) | German *sch* = /ʃ/ | English *sh* would be more typical of American recorders |
| `s` before vowels | German *s* = /z/ in some positions | English reads this as /s/ |

### 5.3 Vowel Length Marking

German linguistics of the period marked vowel length with:
- Vowel doubling (`ee`, `oo`) — shared with English, so ambiguous source
- `h` after vowel (`ah`, `eh`) — German uses *Dehnungs-h* (lengthening h), which is NOT the same as English "ah" for /aː/
- No marking at all — both German and English leave short vowels unmarked

Prince's `ah` spelling could derive from either English "ah" (indicating quality as much as length) or German Dehnungs-h (indicating length primarily). The ambiguity cannot be resolved without acoustic evidence.

### 5.4 German "Sharpening" (Consonant Fortition)

German-influenced recorders sometimes:
- Write initial /b, d, g/ as `b, d, g` but intend fortis articulation
- Apply final devoicing even when hearing voiced finals — writing `d` but hearing /t/
- Use unaspirated `p, t, k` where English recorders would write `b, d, g`

### 5.5 Umlaut-Like Notation

German linguists sometimes used `ü`, `ö`, `ä` when hearing sounds not in English or German. Whether Prince does this in the glossary requires a full search, but it is a known pattern in German-trained linguist transcriptions (e.g., in some Native American linguistic work from this period).

### 5.6 Summary: Orthographic Confusion Index

The glossary presents a three-way orthographic competition:

| Source | Convention | Example | Interpretation |
|--------|-----------|---------|---------------|
| **English colonial** (Williams, Eliot) | `ch` for /tʃ/, `sh` for /ʃ/ | "cheese" → cheese (loan) | Familiar, predictable |
| **English vernacular** (Fielding's diary) | Ad-hoc English-based | Variable spelling | Unpredictable |
| **German linguistic** (Prince's training) | `tsh` for /tʃ/, `z` for /ts/, *Dehnungs-h* | Specific forms | Detectable pattern |

## 6. Consonant /h/ and Preaspiration Evidence

### 6.1 Explicit /h/ in Prince-Speck

One of Prince-Speck's most valuable features for SNEA historical phonology is its treatment of /h/:

| Type | Example | Notes |
|------|---------|-------|
| **Word-initial h** | `ahupanun` (come here) | English recorders often drop word-initial h |
| **Intervocalic h** | `bahduntah` (rising) | Often missed by colonial recorders |
| **Cluster hC** | `bahkeder` (bitter?) | PREASPIRATION — critical for SNEA reconstruction |

### 6.2 Preaspiration (/hk/, /hp/, /ht/)

The spelling `hk` in `bahkeder` is significant because it represents a phonetic feature that English-colonial recorders systematically miss:

- Roger Williams (Narragansett) writes plain `k` for both /k/ and /hk/
- John Eliot (Wampanoag) uses `∞` or doubled vowels to indicate syllable weight but does not write preaspiration
- Ezra Stiles (Mohegan-Pequot) sometimes writes `hk` but inconsistently
- **Prince writes `hk`, `hp`, `ht` clusters explicitly** — this is unique among the pre-modern SNEA sources

### 6.3 Why Prince-Speck Catches Preaspiration

Prince's Germanic training gave him an ear for **aspiration contrasts**. German distinguishes:
- Aspirated /pʰ, tʰ, kʰ/ in certain positions
- Unaspirated /p, t, k/ in clusters
- The *Dehnungs-h* convention made him conscious of h-like elements

Additionally, Mohegan-Pequot may have preserved preaspiration longer than other SNEA languages, or Speck's field notes may have captured it from Fidelia Fielding's careful speech.

### 6.4 Comparison with Other Sources

| Source | Preaspiration /hk/ | Intervocalic /h/ | Word-initial /h/ |
|--------|-------------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Williams 1643 | Not written | Sometimes | Rarely |
| Eliot 1660s | Not written | Rarely | Rarely |
| Stiles 1760s–90s | Inconsistent | Inconsistent | Inconsistent |
| **Prince-Speck 1904** | **Written** | **Written** | **Written** |
| Fielding 2013 | Not phonemic (modern Mohegan lost it) | Not present | Not present |

This makes Prince-Speck a **key witness for preaspiration** in SNEA historical phonology, on par with Mayhew and Bourne (Wampanoag) for the same feature.

Loanwords in the glossary can be identified by:

3. **Orthographic cues:** English spelling patterns that diverge from Mohegan-Pequot patterns

Loanwords should be kept with identity mapping (loanword → same word) rather than pushed through Mohegan-Pequot phoneme conversion. The detection FSM should flag suspected loanwords at runtime based on phonotactic patterns and semantic context.

## 9. Comparison with Stephanie Fielding (2013) Modern Mohegan Dictionary

### 9.1 Overview

Stephanie Fielding (Yôpôwi Yoht, "Morning Fire"), a Mohegan linguist educated at MIT, published the *Modern Mohegan Dictionary* in 2013 and revised in 2015. This is the only modern comprehensive dictionary of Mohegan.

### 9.2 Orthography Comparison

| Feature | Fielding (2013) | Prince-Speck (1904) |
|---------|----------------|---------------------|
| Base | Phonemic (designed) | Phonetic (impressionistic) |
| Vowel length | Explicit | Inconsistent |
| /h/ | Not phonemic | Orthographic |
| Preaspiration | Not marked | Marked |
| Nasalization | Not marked | Not marked |
| Loanwords | Identified | Unidentified |
| Consonant system | Simplified (modern) | More complex (archaic) |

### 9.3 Cognate Mapping for Reconstruction

The pipeline of **Prince-Speck → Fielding → Proto-SNEA** is powerful for reconstruction:

1. **Fielding gives the modern phonemic form** (what the language sounds like now)
2. **Prince-Speck gives the older phonetic form** (what the language sounded like ~110 years ago)
3. **Differences between them indicate sound changes in progress**

For example:
- If Fielding writes `iip` for 'water' and Prince-Speck writes `neep`, the `n-` may have been lost (common Algonquian prefix reduction in modern Mohegan)
- If Fielding lacks preaspiration but Prince-Speck writes `hk`, the preaspiration was lost between 1904 and 2013

### 9.4 Limitations

- Fielding's dictionary is reconstructive — many entries are not from fluent speakers but from archival sources (including Prince-Speck!)
- This introduces circularity: Fielding may have reconstructed a word FROM Prince-Speck, so using Fielding to "verify" Prince-Speck is invalid for that word
- Fielding's dictionary represents the modern revival orthography, which makes different phonemic choices from what Prince-Speck recorded

To use Fielding effectively, we need to know which Fielding entries derive from Prince-Speck (self-source) vs. from other sources (independent verification).

### 10.3 Preaspiration Phonology

Preaspiration in SNEA languages is a debated feature. The Prince-Speck evidence suggests:

- Preaspiration occurred before fortis stops in coda position: *ahk*, *ahp*, *aht*
- It may have been a syllable-level prosodic feature (fortis/lenis distinction) rather than a segment
- Prince-Speck writes it as `hC` sequences, but these may represent single preaspirated segments /ʰC/
- The fact that it appears in Prince-Speck and not in earlier colonial sources may mean: (a) it developed later in Mohegan-Pequot, (b) earlier recorders missed it, or (c) Prince's Germanic ear was better tuned to it

### 10.4 Nasalization

Mohegan-Pequot had nasal vowels (inherited from Proto-Algonquian *ē, *ā, etc.). Prince-Speck:
- Does not systematically mark nasalization
- May occasionally indicate it through vowel quality choices (e.g., `o` for nasalized /ã/)
- May miss it entirely, mapping nasal vowels to oral vowels

Fielding's Mohegan has lost nasal vowels, so the modern source is no help for this feature.
